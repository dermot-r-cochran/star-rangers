Abbess Ilsabet Marrowtide keeps a written record the Crown itself cannot always confirm or contradict, and has spent her tenure making very sure the distinction stays exactly that plain to everyone who asks.

She holds the Abbeylands as tenant-in-chief of the Crown, answerable to Vellamar for one obligation only — the fixed annual grain render her order has delivered without interruption since a founding charter neither the throne nor the Abbey can independently date. Her more consequential duty, by her own account, is the one the charter doesn't mention: keeping the Long Accounting, the Kingdom's sole continuous written archive, in an unbroken hand reaching back further than any royal chronicle the court can recite from memory.

## Known History

- Entered the Tideward Sisterhood as a novice on the Abbeylands' terraces before rising through the order's internal ranks to Abbess.
- Assumed the Long Accounting's keeping on her predecessor's death, continuing an unbroken written record whose origin point neither the Abbey nor the Crown can fully verify.
- Corrected the royal court's own oral chronicle against the Long Accounting on at least two documented points of succession in living memory — disagreements neither side has pressed to formal resolution.
- Became the Survey Corps' primary Kingdom-side contact for archival and linguistic matters following first contact, on the strength of the Long Accounting being the only written record available in a language the Corps' own delegation was still working to translate.

## Character Notes

Ilsabet's authority runs almost entirely through the written record rather than through court standing, and she has made a point of keeping it that way — Aduine's Council-elected steward administers the island's practical governance, the Crown holds sovereignty, and the Abbess holds nothing but the Abbeylands' terraces and the Long Accounting, a deliberately narrow portfolio she has declined more than one Council invitation to expand.

She met the Survey Corps' first standing delegation with the Long Accounting itself rather than with any formal greeting, on the reasoning that a written record predating the Corps' own arrival by longer than either institution could confirm was the more honest introduction to make. Delegation linguists working through the Kingdom's presumed-extinct language have called her patience with their translation errors nearly bottomless; Ilsabet's own explanation is that a Sisterhood which has kept one language's record unbroken for generations understands better than most institutions how much a single mistranslation can cost a written archive's credibility, and would rather correct a delegation slowly than let a bad translation stand uncorrected in either record.

She is careful, in every exchange with outside visitors, to distinguish what the Long Accounting actually states from what court tradition merely assumes — a habit that has made her simultaneously the delegation's most reliable Kingdom source and, by her own wry admission, the Crown's least comfortable one.
