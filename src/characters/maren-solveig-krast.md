General Krast gave the Military Space Command its most durable line in a single unscripted answer, and spent the rest of her career discovering that a line durable enough to survive an institution's collapse does not stay owned by the person who said it.

Krast rose through Solar Command's officer track on Mars during the MSC's middle decades, when the institution's technocratic self-confidence was at its least examined. She held a series of engineering-command postings before her appointment to the Outer Stations Consolidation Hearing panel in 2689 UCSD — a hearing convened, nominally, to review escalating grievances from outer-station and deep-range habitat delegations about the MSC's advisory-only council structure.

## Known History

- Born 2631 UCSD, commissioned into Solar Command's engineering-track officer corps in her twenties.
- Held command postings across several Mars-adjacent infrastructure programmes before her appointment to general rank.
- Addressed the Outer Stations Consolidation Hearing in 2689 UCSD, delivering the line MSC culture would go on to teach as defensible institutional memory: *"We tried democracy. The result was forty years of the Coherence Wars and three billion dead. I would rather be called a tyrant than repeat the experiment."*
- Continued in senior Solar Command postings on Mars until her death in 2714 UCSD, nine years before the Consolidation Accords restructured the MSC into the Solar System Defence Command.
- Did not live to see her own line's reception shift from institutional doctrine to a cautionary quotation SSDC circles no longer always repeat as approval.

## Character Notes

Krast's line reads, out of context, as the kind of thing said for effect. The hearing record suggests otherwise: she gave the answer unscripted, in response to a delegate's direct question about why the MSC would not convert its consultative councils into binding ones, and meant every word of it exactly as delivered. She was not being provocative. She was being, by her own lights, honest about a trade she believed the Solar System had already made once and could not survive making badly a second time.

What her line left out — deliberately or not, the hearing record does not resolve which — was the argument her outer-station critics had already made and would keep making for another quarter-century: that the Coherence Wars were not caused by democracy in the abstract, but by democratic systems captured and destabilised by interests that could not survive real accountability. Krast had the historical facts of the wars correct. She did not, on the surviving record, ever directly answer that narrower counter-argument, and it is not clear she considered it a different claim from the one she'd already refuted to her own satisfaction.

She remains, within Solar Command's institutional memory, a genuinely respected engineering officer whose one hearing-room answer outlived her entire career and came, eventually, to mean something closer to the opposite of what she intended by it.
