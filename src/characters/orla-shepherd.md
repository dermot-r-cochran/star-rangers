Órla Shepherd has spent forty local years keeping a rule she was never given the reason for, and one afternoon in 2831 UCSD, her own daughter gave it to her anyway.

She holds the Shepherd croft in the Boirinn Uplands the way her mother held it before her and her mother's mother before that — a cooperative flock-holding worked across ground too stony and too wet for row crops, grazing livestock descended from Currach Fleet stock on land that has answered to the same family for six generations. She has never left Tír na nÓg, has no interest in doing so, and has said as much to her daughter more than once without either woman finding it a point worth arguing over.

## Known History

- Sixth-generation holder of the Shepherd croft's grazing rights in the Boirinn Uplands.
- Raised Tissadelle Shepherd on the croft before her daughter's departure for Star Ranger training.
- Maintains the family's unexplained Hollow customs — clear water, uncut pale wood, no lingering past dusk — inherited without a stated reason across six generations of the croft's women.
- Received her daughter, then a Line Captain, on personal leave in 2831 UCSD following a Union data-exchange delivery at the council house, and was told — off any Ranger record, as family rather than as report — what the Hollow's rule actually protects against.

## Character Notes

Órla's patience with unexplained rules has always been practical rather than reverent: she calls the Hollow's customs "a habit worth keeping regardless" of whether anyone can say why, and has raised her daughter to notice the difference between a rule kept out of respect and a rule kept because nobody has yet had a reason to break it. That practicality is exactly what let her recognise, within seconds, that the trained stillness her daughter brought to the edge of the Hollow was not homesickness.

She reads her daughter's sentences the way she reads weather — Tissadelle stopped saying *I think* and started saying *I haven't confirmed that yet* in her second year of training, and Órla has tracked the shape of every letter since for exactly that kind of small, exact change. She has told herself, with only partial success, that exactness is not the same as distance.

She did not ask her daughter to break Ranger protocol for her. She asked a narrower question — *you're not going to write this down* — and left room for Tissadelle to answer it as family rather than as Line Captain. When the answer came anyway, unprompted and unfiled, Órla did not treat it as a small thing. Six generations kept the rule without the reason. She is the first to be offered a choice about which one she'd rather have, and chose the reason, walking, with the light going and no patience left to spend standing still past dusk to debate it.
