Rhian Gwynne has spent twenty local years designing systems for worlds she has never set foot on, and considers that entirely normal for the job.

She works out of Ynys Wydrin's dome engineering base, the Celtic Union's shared fabrication and life-support development centre — a posting that exists precisely because Ynys Wydrin's own survival margins are thin enough to force real engineering rigor, in a way a more forgiving world never would have. Her specialty is recycler architecture and cold-world agricultural adaptation, two fields the Union has exported from Ynys Wydrin's base to charter worlds settled generations after her own.

## Known History

- Trained at the dome engineering base under the same apprenticeship track her mother completed a generation earlier.
- Led the recycler redesign credited with extending domed-township service intervals across Ynys Wydrin's own settlements by a documented margin, before her design work began travelling further than she did.
- Adapted cold-world agricultural methods for export to two later Celtic Union charter worlds, coordinating entirely through data exchange and without personal travel to either.
- Maintains an ongoing technical correspondence with cooperative engineers on both recipient worlds, none of whom she has met in person.

## Character Notes

Rhian has been asked more than once, usually by visiting Union delegates surprised to learn it, why she has never travelled to see her own designs running on the worlds that adopted them. Her answer is consistent and, colleagues say, entirely sincere: Ynys Wydrin's own domes need her more than a finished installation elsewhere does, and a design that only works with its original engineer standing over it was not actually finished when she exported it.

She treats "borrowed sun" — the affectionate Ynys Wydrin term for the engineered light spectrum piped through the domes' composite canopies — as the working proof of her own design philosophy: a system good enough to stop being noticed by the people living under it. She has said, more than once and not entirely joking, that she'll consider a design finished only once its recipient world stops writing to thank her and starts writing to complain about routine maintenance instead — the surest sign, in her estimation, that a system has become ordinary infrastructure rather than a visiting engineer's project.

She keeps a working file of every fault report either recipient world has ever sent her, not as a record of failure but as the closest thing she has to a shared logbook with engineers she may never meet. She answers every one personally, usually within the day.
