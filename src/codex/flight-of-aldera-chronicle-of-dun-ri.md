**COMPOSITE TEXT — RECITED CHRONICLE**

**Current standing:** Not part of the Survey Archive's confirmed historical record. Recited across multiple Fellowship of Light chapters in church-space, with no single credited author and no two variants matching in every particular. Filed here as a cultural/legendary source, cross-checkable against canon the way any codex document is — not as settled fact about who Elvira and Aldera were, or whether "were" is even the right tense.

---

The light in the walled terrace at Castle Ravana came in low and sideways, dragging long shadows across frost-blackened grass and the last roses, which had no business still blooming. The air tasted of iron. Princess Elvira moved along the gravel path with the measured tread of someone being watched, her gloved fingers pressing the balustrade at intervals, testing, though she already knew every loose rivet. Her cat moved with her — not following, not leading, but orbiting, as though tethered by something other than loyalty.

The Abbey crouched on the hillside above the River Boyne, older than the castles, older than the kings who'd built them. Elvira had braced herself for incense and chanting. Instead, before the gates were even open, the sound found her — not the clang of iron on iron but something below that, a frequency that lived in bone rather than air. Aldera went rigid in her arms, ears pinned flat, and then, disturbingly, stopped moving altogether.

"Princess Elvira." The monk materialized from the smithy's orange glow — tonsured, soot-dark, his hands the hands of someone who worked metal and knew what it cost. "I am Brother Daire. We've been expecting you."

"Expecting me?"

"Your cat announced you three days ago. The animals always know first." He turned toward the chapel, where the stained glass — Celtic knotwork strangling saints, serpents devouring their own tails — seized the afternoon light and broke it into something that made the old blood run in your mother's line. The old blood. The Fellowship has maintained records.

He led her through corridors where stone gave way to wood, wood to something that might have been bone or bleached coral, arranged in spirals that seemed to shift when she looked directly at them. The library occupied the entire eastern wing, floor to ceiling shelves, yes, but also workbenches covered in instruments she couldn't name, glass vessels containing liquids that moved against gravity, and manuscripts. Thousands of them.

"Christian texts for the Church's eyes," Daire said, touching a leather-bound Gospel. "And these—" he slid aside a panel to reveal shelves of vellum covered in Ogham script and intricate diagrams that hurt to look at, "—for those who need to know how the world actually works."

He pulled down a codex, opened it to a page showing a spiral galaxy rendered in illumination that seemed to rotate. "Your restlessness. The metallic taste in autumn air. The sense of being watched." He tapped the page. "You're not mad, Princess. You're sensitive to the Overfold. The boundaries are thin here, and growing thinner."

## Dún Rí

The light in the walled terrace at Castle Ravana came aslant, rolling over the frost-dulled grass and the stubborn petals of last-chance roses. You could taste autumn in the air — bright, bitter, mineral. Princess Elvira liked to pace the gravel walk at this hour, trailing her gloved fingertips over the ironwork balustrade, pretending she was not being watched from the high windows. Her white-furred cat trailed her, a constant shadow, six paces behind or three paces ahead, always in motion, never content.

Aldera moved with a deliberate hitch in her step. The misstep from last night's leap still ached, but she wore the limp like armor, a reason to refuse being scooped up, stroked, soothed. Her gaze — unnervingly green in the golden afternoon — remained fixed on Elvira's boots as they traced their rigid path through the rose corridor. They rounded the sundial; Elvira stopped abruptly, as if catching a distant sound. She always did this — the pause, the sideways tilt of her head, the slow exhale through pursed lips, as though steeling herself for a blow. Aldera had learned to read it: Elvira sensed something too, some vibration in the air that prickled the skin. But she would never admit it. The princess who feared nothing could not acknowledge fear.

Aldera didn't wait. She closed the gap in three quick strides, claws unsheathed, batting at Elvira's skirts.

"Mmmrrrow." The sound carried weight. A warning. A demand.

Elvira's brow furrowed, irritation flickering between her carefully shaped eyebrows. "Again? You've had your outing, your meal, a pillow in every chamber."

She crouched, knees creaking under the tailored velvet of her day dress, and caught Aldera behind the head. Held her firm, thumb and forefinger pinching just enough to remind Aldera who was in charge. She forced Aldera's face up until their gazes met — so close Aldera could smell the sugar on Elvira's breath, the faint astringency of her cosmetics.

"There's nothing here. Nothing coming," Elvira whispered, low enough for only them to hear. "Go amuse yourself. Or better, sleep. I have enough on my mind."

Aldera lashed out, claws grazing Elvira's hand — not hard enough to draw blood, but enough to make her point. Elvira released her with a sharp inhale.

Then the mute footman appeared, his face as expressionless as carved ivory, gloves already loosened at the wrists. He offered Elvira a nod before bending to collect Aldera, his hands efficient, impersonal. No affection. No hesitation. Just removal.

"Out, then," Elvira said, already rising and dusting herself off. She gave a perfunctory wave and turned away, leaving Aldera to the care of the mute.

At the garden gate — tall, wrought iron, its bars tipped with rust and the brittle remains of rose thorns — the footman set Aldera down and secured the latch with a resonant clang. She stood motionless, tail twitching with restraint. She pressed her forehead against the cold metal, watching Elvira's retreating figure, her hips swaying with controlled frustration.

A pause. A single breath, maybe two. Then Aldera turned away from Dún Rí and its watchful windows, from the sentries in their polished helmets, toward the sweeping lawns and the hazy outline of the city below. She limped, but it wasn't just pain that stiffened her gait — it was the mix of fear and fierce, unfamiliar exhilaration.

The wind struck her, colder beyond the terrace's shelter. The city's scents — tanneries, spices, sweat — hit like a physical force after the curated air of the rose garden. Aldera moved swiftly, head low, ignoring the shouts of children and the shrill cries of vendors hawking nuts and candied fruit. The wind carried the salt-stink of the harbor, and beneath it, the particular rot of fish guts and tar that meant Port Láirge. She didn't know the route, but her nose did.

By the time she reached the harbor wall, her limp had become a hop, but she didn't slow. The sky darkened, clouds massing like bruises over the water. Aldera tested the air — salt, fish, and beneath it, something sharp, electric. She scaled a stack of empty crates, surveying the docks. Merchants shouted at stevedores, sails snapped in the wind, a child's sobs echoed somewhere behind her. No one noticed the white cat, now smudged with dirt and blood from her torn paw, as she slipped between the planks and onto the first ship that carried the scent of earth, of stale bread and spilled cider.

She concealed herself in the galley, beneath a slop bucket. The cook — a broad woman missing a thumb — discovered her once and aimed a kick that missed by a hair's breadth. "Rats we have," she announced, "not cats." But Aldera saw the way she left the galley door ajar — too lazy to latch it properly, or too tired from the day's work to care. The fish heads came with a grunt of effort, not kindness, but they came. Tolerated, not wanted. That was enough.

When the ship groaned free of its moorings and swung into the dark water, Aldera felt the last remnants of Dún Rí dissolve. The open sea filled her lungs with its cold, briny breath. She crouched at the prow as the city's lights blurred into a golden haze, then vanished. Above her, the mast groaned, the sails hummed, and for the first time she could recall, no wall, no hedge, no hand could claim her.

She huddled into herself, licking at the split pads of her paw. The pain was sharp, but sharpness felt honest in a way that palace comfort never did. The world was bigger than the terrace, bigger than Elvira's clipped admonitions. On this deck, under a sky that felt less and less familiar, Aldera had no name except her own.

The night watch spotted her, of course. One slurred her name — "Ghost" — his voice thick with drink. Another dangled salted meat, but when she refused, he spat and muttered of ill omens. The third, older, with a sea serpent tattoo coiled up his arm, simply watched. Then, with two crooked fingers, he saluted. Old sailors' code — a gesture for a ship's cat who'd earned her place, or for any creature that had survived what should have killed it. Aldera held his gaze and didn't look away.

Night. The horizon gone, the sky an empty vault, the only light was the ship's lantern swinging at the stern. The deck rolled and flexed under her, but she learned the rhythm fast. Each pitch and yaw became part of her. There was no comfort, but there was meaning.

By the time the first pale light knifed up from the east, Aldera had chosen her path. The old city was just a memory, a bruise you could not lick away. The cold, the hunger, the blood in her mouth — these were hers now. She watched the sun rise, knowing that somewhere, behind a wall of roses and silence, Elvira would be searching the terrace, calling her name in that cracked, tired voice. She felt nothing, except the urge to survive.

And as the ship cut through the channel, leaving the world she knew behind, Aldera turned her back on everything soft and familiar. She was her own master now, and the voyage had only just begun.

## Scene Two

By morning, the sea heaved with the slow rhythm of a sleeping giant. The merchant ship rolled close to the island — a spined crescent of wet stone and salt grass, slick with morning mist and the gray shudder of early light. The air was sharp with iodine, and each breath burned a little. Aldera abandoned her post under the slop bucket long before the anchor groaned, choosing instead to climb onto the low gunwale and stare at the unwelcoming shore, trying to ignore the hollow ache that curled and gnawed under her ribs.

The sailors had not fed her, not even the barest scraps, and the cook's accidental fish heads were long gone. She could smell the promise of life on the wind: the faint musk of seabirds, the cold metallic tinge of tide pool things writhing beneath stones. The world was louder here. The chitter of gulls, the hiss and slap of waves, the barely-audible conversations of the island's invisible denizens. Aldera's world had always been curated: meals at regular intervals, fresh linens, predictable human routine. Here, in the open, hunger was not a passing discomfort but a purpose, a screaming imperative.

The ship's plank thudded onto rock, and the first mate — narrow-eyed, voice like a splitting rope — swung her unceremoniously from the deck. "Go on, then. We don't need bad luck."

Aldera landed on the rounded stones with a thump that jarred her teeth. The crew watched, faces unreadable, as she shook off the impact and glared up at them. One man crossed himself. Another spat — a dark cat at sea, some said, drew storms, or worse, carried the dead's voices in their throats. When the plank retracted, the hull's shadow swept over her, leaving a cold patch where her body had just begun to warm.

No instructions, no comforts, not even a glance back as the ship lurched away, its sails bellied with wind.

Aldera surveyed her new domain. The island was all knife-edge ridges and drowned coves, with the remains of someone's careless campsite — ash, scattered fish bones, a loop of old rope — just above the tide line. She tested her paw, flexed it until the pain leveled off into background noise, and limped toward the nearest cluster of black basalt. The cold settled into her bones, but the adrenaline of pure necessity kept her moving.

She tried the first pool. Water clear as glass, winking with anemones and the darting glint of tiny, impossibly fast shrimp. She pawed at them, claws snicking through water, but they vanished between rocks before she could even process the motion. She lowered her head to drink but the salt stung her tongue, leaving her more desperate than before.

Next, a fissure near the base of a twisted juniper, promising cover from the gusting wind. Inside, nothing but a ring of shell fragments and the reek of old bird droppings. No human comfort. No memory of home.

Aldera mewled, softly at first, then with a growing edge of panic. She called out the way she would when lost in the palace, trying to summon someone, anyone. She pressed herself against the stone, claws scraping for purchase, tried to climb, to find her own way out — but the rock face offered nothing. The sound died quick against the stone, lost in the clatter of a distant wave. She tried again. This time, something answered: a long, uninflected growl, low and level as a cello.

She froze. Ears canted back, heart a spike in her chest.

From the mist-shrouded rim of the cove, a shape detached itself — a cat, but unlike any Aldera had ever seen. Lean, nearly twice her length, pelt dappled with the umber of autumn leaves and the white scarring of old fights. Its eyes burned a flat, uncanny amber. A lynx. Wild, beautiful, and, at this distance, possibly lethal.

Aldera pressed herself into the shadow of the rock, unsure whether to confront or cower. The lynx advanced, casual, no hurry. The tip of its tail flicked once, twice. When it halted, the two stared each other down, meters apart, sharing only breath and the ancestral knowledge that all cats are both predators and prey.

The lynx dropped its haunches, sat, and regarded Aldera with a slow blink. Then it spoke — not in words, but in a guttural vibration that said, "You are not from here."

Aldera didn't move. The lynx's ears twitched, then it looked away and began to groom its left paw with calculated indifference. Message received: You are no threat. Yet.

Minutes passed. The sun never quite pierced the clouds, but the island brightened to a monochrome haze. Aldera's hunger refused to fade. She inched closer to the nearest tide pool, never letting the lynx out of her peripheral vision. She tried again for the shrimp; missed, and nearly soaked herself. The lynx watched, then stalked forward, not to attack, but to claim a spot at the edge of the pool. With a single, fluid motion, it plunged a foreleg, withdrew, and tossed a limp crab onto the stones.

It crunched the crab with a practiced precision, then pushed the remains toward Aldera with a paw.

A test.

Aldera stepped in, nose wrinkling at the briny, almost medicinal stink. She crunched the legs, felt the pop of carapace between her molars. It hurt her split lip but the taste — raw, sweet, full of alien vigor — was unlike anything she'd ever known. She finished the offering, licked her paw, and sat back. The lynx met her gaze. A silent agreement: for now, they would share this space.

The next hours were a study in desperation and adaptation. The lynx led, showing by example: how to search for eggs in the sand, how to use a quick feint to scare minnows into the shallows, how to find the trickle of rainwater collected in the hollowed stone. Aldera failed more often than she succeeded. The minnow slipped through her teeth and battered her face as it thrashed. The eggs, when she finally unearthed them, burst too easily, dribbling viscous gold onto her paw. But she watched, she learned, and she grew faster.

Mid-morning, the two cats found themselves at the same rivulet, lapping side by side. The lynx finished first, shook water from its whiskers, and brushed against Aldera's shoulder. A strange resonance passed through her — a vibration that lived in bone rather than fur, like the hum of the Abbey smithy but softer, intimate. The lynx's cells sang at a frequency her own body answered without understanding. The gesture wasn't affection, but it was contact, something beyond the blank indifference of the palace or the brutal hierarchy of the ship's deck.

They foraged together, growing bolder as hunger receded. Twice, the lynx allowed Aldera to lead; once, Aldera chased off a pair of inquisitive crows with a yowl so fierce even she surprised herself. The sun threatened to break through at noon, only to be swallowed by a resurgence of cloud. They pressed into the driftwood log together, the windbreak a meager but sufficient shield, and huddled close as the temperature dropped.

By late day, the lynx slept, its body rising and falling with a deep, unworried calm. Aldera could not rest — not yet. She licked her fur clean, tending the ragged paw and the nicked ear, cataloging the day's hurts and the victories. The island was no longer entirely alien. She'd marked her territory, learned the rules. And in the creature beside her, she saw not a rival, but a pattern — a shape to follow, even, perhaps, a companion.

Tomorrow would be harder. There were larger predators here, and storms, and the slow, grinding threat of starvation. But Aldera felt something rare and difficult ignite in her chest: the beginnings of pride. She curled herself against the lynx's flank and let the thrum of its breathing draw her down, not into sleep, but into a vigilant, predator's rest.

The exile was no longer a punishment. It was a test, and Aldera was determined to succeed.

## Scene Three

The day died slowly in Dún Rí. Its last light puddled along the meditation chamber's tatami, then vanished, leaving only the roseate shimmer of lanterns and the somber procession of shadows along the lacquered walls. Incense bled from the mouth of a bronze jaguar, curling its blue smoke into the gloom. Princess Elvira knelt on the low bench, hands folded in her lap, each finger trembling in time with the pulse at her throat.

She had not slept since Aldera vanished. The hours that followed had been a long, blunt instrument — too dull to cut but heavy enough to bruise. She paced the empty rooms, called out in hallways so silent her voice came back doubled. The household grew nervous. Servants dropped their eyes, answered in short bursts, retreated from her as if afraid of her anger.

Even now, alone in the dim, Elvira felt the castle pressing in. The walls, once familiar, now seemed full of corners where memory festered. She stared at the lacquered screen before her — a triptych of feline silhouettes, gold leaf and indigo, each cat in a different pose: one stalking, one poised, one crouched in readiness. A token of comfort, perhaps, or a taunt.

Behind her, the chamber doors rasped open and the household sage entered on bare feet. He wore white robes, simple as flour, and a circlet of iron braided into his sparse hair. He did not bow but inclined his head, eyes flickering over the ritual arrangement: a perfect circle of river stones, a tray of unlit candles, a small hand mirror set precisely at the circle's north.

"You are certain?" he asked, not even a greeting.

"I am." Elvira's voice was hoarse. "Begin when you are ready."

The sage struck the stones with a short rod; they sang, a strange chime that seemed to coil inward rather than outward. He lit the candles in clockwise order, lips moving in a silent recitation. As the third wick caught, the incense thickened, filling Elvira's mouth with the taste of rain and smoke.

She closed her eyes, breathed, tried to forget the ache that ran from her jaw to her heart. She pictured Aldera — not as the memory, but as she had been in the moment of departure: fur soiled, eyes wild, limping but unbroken. She forced the image forward, past the comforting hallucinations of Aldera curled at her feet, into the cold, alien domain beyond the castle walls.

The bell sounded — a bronze note, pure and slightly flat. The sage's voice, low and full of dust, intoned: "Let what is lost find its path. Let the barrier thin."

Elvira let herself tip into the ritual. The room faded; the darkness behind her eyelids was not void but a teeming field of sense, a tapestry of half-remembered touches, the pressure of Aldera's small skull against her palm, the tickle of whiskers, the prick of her claws.

Then, nothing.

Time compressed, expanded, lost meaning.

A sound, wet and raw, like a cat's tongue scraping bone.

And suddenly, in the darkness behind her eyes, she saw a flash of white fur streaking across wet stone, followed by a larger shadow — amber-eyed, impossible, beautiful. Aldera? Or the memory of her? The distinction blurred.

Elvira reached for the image, desperate for even an echo of contact. In her lap, her hands clenched. The stones vibrated. The bell rang again, twice, once for each soul.

A sharp cold invaded her, real as steel. Elvira gasped and opened her eyes. The incense had burned low, its final thread curling up and dissolving into the lantern-lit gloom.

She stared at the mirror. For an instant, she saw what she needed: a single, perfect paw print, smudged on the glass as if pressed from the other side. Impossible, but there. The shape of Aldera's pads, unmistakable.

Her chest seized; tears stung, unwelcome.

She reached out, touched the print. It was cold, then gone. The surface reflected only her own face — eyes red, jaw set with an anger born of self-disgust.

The sage watched her. "You found her?"

Elvira drew in a breath. "She is alive." Her voice did not tremble.

The sage inclined his head, ritual complete. "Then the next move is yours, Princess."

She nodded once, fierce and unsmiling, and swept the stones into her pocket. She would not wait for fate, or for her pet to find her way home. She would seek, she would call, she would tear the world apart if she had to.

When she stood, the bench toppled behind her. She left it that way, a small monument to the life that, until tonight, had been only balance and show.

The search would begin at dawn.

*Archivist's note (Fellowship comparative archive, cross-chapter submission): Filed as a recited chronicle, not a document — no chapter has produced a manuscript master, and the handful of transcriptions on record disagree on details no two reciters seem to consider important enough to reconcile: Aldera's coat is dark, then white, within the same telling in some variants; "Castle Ravana" and "Dún Rí" are used for what context suggests is one seat, not two, in every version collected so far. We have declined to silently correct any of this. The pattern matches what this Archive already notes for other unattributed oral pieces — see [Hymn of the Thin Places](/star-rangers/codex/hymn-of-the-thin-places/) — where the variance is treated by reciters as part of what the piece is about, not a transmission error to be fixed.*

*Two open questions this Archive is not in a position to close. First: whether the "Brother Daire" of this telling is the same Brother Daire who set down [The Life of Saint Aoife](/star-rangers/codex/life-of-saint-aoife/) at Cill Aoife in 1216 AD, a later namesake in the same tradition, or a title rather than a personal name within the smithy-and-scriptorium role this Abbey keeps. The record that would settle it has not surfaced. Second, and more pointed: whether this era's Princess Elvira and her cat Aldera bear any relation — descent, namesake, or something this Archive does not yet have a word for — to the Elvira and Aldera of the Threshold Station survey era. No genealogy, transit record, or Cascade-tier account connects them. Until one does, this Archive holds the two pairs as distinct, coincidentally named individuals, on the same principle that keeps the Corps' Field Triumvirate distinct from [Kieme](/star-rangers/glossary/kieme/)'s legacy name: a shared name is not shared descent, and the Archive's job is to say so plainly rather than let the coincidence do uncredited narrative work.*

*"The Overfold" does not appear as a term anywhere in the Survey Archive's own technical record. Read against [the Five Layers](/star-rangers/lore/five-layers/) and [boundary zone](/star-rangers/glossary/boundary-zone/) taxonomy, Brother Daire's diagnosis — restlessness, a metallic taste in the air, the sense of being watched, worsening as "the boundaries" thin — describes a boundary-zone-adjacent sensitivity in every particular this Archive can check. Nothing in the account requires anything beyond that. The Abbey had no instruments by that name for what it was pointing at; it had vellum, Ogham, and a smith-monk who called it by the nearest word his tradition gave him.*
