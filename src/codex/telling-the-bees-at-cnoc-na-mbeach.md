The message came in on the rota's own channel, three lines long, the way they always come: name, posting, cause. Rigger First Class Deiniol Vance, Kernowek Reach frontier survey, hull breach during an unscheduled fold-drift correction. No fault assigned. No fault to assign. I had never met him. Neither had Fintan. It didn't matter.

He read the three lines twice, standing at the hive-yard gate with the message still open on the slate, and said nothing for long enough that I thought he hadn't heard me arrive. Then he set the slate down on the fence post, face up, as though the hives themselves might want to read it, and went to fetch the black cloth.

I have been on this rota two years and had heard the custom described before I ever saw it done. Knowing the shape of a thing and watching a man old enough to have three decorations for boundary readings that held under conditions that should have broken them do it without a trace of self-consciousness are not the same knowledge. He tied the cloth to the first hive's roof-slat, and spoke to it — not loudly, not performed for my benefit, the exact register a man uses to tell a colleague something they need to know and nothing more.

"Deiniol Vance," he said. "Not one of ours. Died doing the same work I did, on a frontier post same as any I stood. You're to know it."

He moved to the second hive and said it again, word for word. Then the third. I asked him, once he'd finished the row, whether the wording had to be exact.

"No," he said. "But I've found I do better keeping to one form than making a new one up each time. Saves me deciding, mid-telling, whether I'm doing it right." He did not look at me when he said this. He was watching the hive entrances, the traffic of it, the way he must have watched an instrument reading for thirty-one years before he watched anything else for a living.

I asked whether he thought it did anything.

"I don't know that it does," he said, "any more than I know a boundary reading confirms itself for the reasons the manual says it does. I know what happens to a colony informed and a colony not, over enough seasons to trust the pattern. That's the whole of what I'll claim for it." He straightened up from the last hive, wiped his hands on his coat, and added, without much ceremony: "I'd rather under-claim it and keep doing it than over-claim it and have to explain myself to someone who's right to ask."

We stood at the fence a while after, the light going, the hum from the row settling back into whatever a settled hive's hum sounds like when nothing further needs telling it. I asked, because the rota trains you to ask the plain question rather than the polite one, whether he still thought about the boundary work — whether a night like this made him miss reading real instruments instead of talking to insects that couldn't answer back.

He was quiet long enough that I thought I'd overstepped.

"I read this the same way," he said finally. "Watch closely. Disturb as little as you can manage. Don't mistake your own certainty about what's inside a thing for what's actually inside it." He nodded once at the row, as if that settled something between him and it that had nothing to do with me. "I never stopped reading instruments. I just found one that stings back if I get impatient with it, which is more than the old ones ever did."

He asked after Vance's people before I left — whether they'd been told properly, whether there was a service the Communion could stand for, whether anyone was going out to the Reach to carry it. There generally is, I told him. There generally is someone. He said that was good, and went back to the wax-house to start on the next batch of candles, because three of the way-chapels on the circuit were low and the rota doesn't wait on grief to keep its own schedule, any more than a hive does.
