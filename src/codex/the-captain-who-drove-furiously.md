**TRANSCRIBED FROM THE COMMISSIONING RECORD, EDEN — 2831 UCSD**
**Spoken by Sister Ione Marrec, of the contemplative chapter, when a cadet asked her a question after the singing**
**Set down at her request in the cadet's words as much as her own, so the question keeps its place in it**

---

They had sung the [Hymn of the Thin Places](/star-rangers/codex/hymn-of-the-thin-places/) at the end, the way a commissioning always closes it, and the cadet — newly sworn, still holding her folded certificate like it might blow away — came to me at the back of the hall while the others went for the food.

"Sister. The song." She was careful about it, the way you're careful about a thing you don't want to be rude about. "It's about a man on a mountain. A prophet. Off Earth, from before any of this. Nobody in that room worships his god. I don't. So why is it *our* song?"

I told her it was a fair question, because it is, and that the honest answer is a longer story than a hallway, but that she'd asked at the right end of it, and I'd give her the short of it if she'd take the short of it.

She said she would.

---

"There was a witness," I said. "On the world the song comes from, long before anyone left it. He lived beside a royal house that had gone rotten — a crown that had made room for a cult that suited it and, with the cult, a rule that answered to no one. Everyone could see the rot. Nobody with a title would name it, because the naming cost more than the silence. So the witness named it. He named it out loud, at a contest he set up on a mountain precisely so nobody could pretend they hadn't heard. And for the naming the crown came after him, and he ran, and he ran so far and so tired he asked to die of it. And then he was taken off the record before the thing he'd named ever came true.

"That's the shape the song is about. Not his god. His *shape*. We keep a whole archive of that shape — a witness who names the boundary the powerful won't, is opposed for it, and is gone before the naming is proved. We found it on so many worlds that never met each other that we stopped being able to call it a coincidence. We file it under one word so we don't have to pick whose version is the real one. The song names the shape and leaves the god to whoever brought the god. That's why a room that believes ten different things can still sing it over the same grave."

The cadet turned that over. "All right," she said. "But the song's only the prophet. You said there was a captain."

"I did. Because the story the song comes out of doesn't end with the witness. It never does — the naming has to be *carried out* by somebody, and the witness is already gone. So the story hands the work to a soldier. A captain. And this is the part the song leaves out, and the part I think you actually asked about."

---

"The captain wasn't a witness," I said. "He was the *answer* to the witness — the hand that does what the naming demanded. He came for the rotten house driving so hard the watchmen knew him by it from a long way off; the corpus remembers him for exactly that, *he drives furiously*. And he did the thing. He ended the house root and branch. He put down the cult so completely they said he'd scoured it out of the land. He built something new where it had stood. By every measure of the naming, he was *right*, and he was thorough, and he meant it.

"And then the same book that praises him will not let him keep the whole of it. Because the rotten house had two idols — the new one the witness had named, and an older one the whole nation had lived with so long it had stopped looking like an idol at all. The captain tore down the new one. The old one he left standing. He couldn't see it, or he could and it was useful, the book doesn't fully say which. And so the thing he founded to end the rot carried the deeper rot in it from the first day. He got his house a fixed span — the book even counts it for you, four generations — and at the end of the count it was narrowed out of the world by the ones who came after, having become, at its own root, the unaccountable thing it had risen up to punish."

The cadet had stopped thinking about the food. "That's not a hero story," she said.

"No. It's a truer one. It's the story of a man who was completely right about the house he pulled down and completely blind to the one he built. And I'll tell you why I tell it to cadets, on the day, in this habitat and not another."

---

She waited.

"You were sworn today into a corps that exists," I said, "because someone once stood in a hearing much like a mountain contest and named a rot everyone could see and no title would touch — that the old command had gone slow and unanswerable and would not close the gap in time. And you were sworn in the shadow of a different figure, a general, whose one honest sentence founded the order the corps replaced. She said she would rather be called a tyrant than try democracy again after it had failed so badly. And she was *right about the rotten house*. The thing before her truly had collapsed; the wars truly had happened. She tore that down and meant every word.

"And she kept the older idol. The distrust of ever being answerable to the people she governed — she couldn't see it, because her whole institution had lived with it so long it had stopped looking like an idol. So the order she founded to end one failure carried the deeper one in it from the first day, and got its span, and at the end of the span was narrowed into its successor by the very pressure it had spent its life refusing to answer. Same shape. The captain's shape. I don't have to tell you her name. You already put it in the story yourself, three sentences ago — I watched you do it."

The cadet said, after a moment, that she had.

"Then here is the only thing I actually came over to say," I told her. "The witnesses in the general's own time — the ones who named, at the time and to no use, that it was never democracy that failed but democracy *captured* — they lost the room. They were out-argued and sent home. And the thing that finally proved them right was the general's own word, turned around in the mouths of the people who came after her, long after the namers were gone. That's the shape too. The naming comes true through the hand that fought it, after the one who named it can't see it anymore.

"You will be, in your career, the witness on the mountain some days and the captain driving furiously on others. The song is only for the first. Nobody writes a song for the second — you have to keep the second yourself, quietly, so that on the day you're right about the house you're pulling down, you remember to look for the older idol you're about to carry in with you. That's not in the hymn. That's the part I stand at the back of the hall to hand you by mouth."

She looked at the certificate in her hand for a while. Then she asked me the captain's name, in the old corpus, so she could keep it.

I told her. She wrote it on the back of the certificate, under her own new commission, where she'd see it.
