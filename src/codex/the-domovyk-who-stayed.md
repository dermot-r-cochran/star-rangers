**THE DOMOVYK WHO STAYED**
*From TALES FROM THE OLD HEARTH, Book One*

---

When the Marchenko family left, they took the icons, the good knives, and the seed jar their great-grandmother had carried through two wars without once letting it break. They did not take the domovyk, because you cannot take a domovyk. A domovyk is not luggage. A domovyk is the house's own memory of being lived in, and a memory does not fold into a transit case.

So he stayed.

He was not sad about it, not at first, because he did not yet understand what leaving meant. He swept the ash from the cold hearth out of habit. He kept the mice from the grain that was no longer there. He listened, the way he always had, for the sound of the door — and for a long time he told himself the family had only gone to the far field, or to the market in the next valley, and would be back by supper with their boots muddy and their arms full.

The seasons did not wait for him to finish believing that. Snow came and left the roof without anyone to clear it, and one beam gave under the weight, and the domovyk mended what he could reach and grieved, quietly, over what he couldn't. Grass grew up through the floor of the good room. A fox denned under the porch two winters running and the domovyk let her, because an empty house is at least less empty with a fox in it.

He counted the years the way houses do — not in numbers, but in what the walls remembered doing. The year of the leaning roof. The year of the fox. The year the well finally went dry and he stopped expecting the bucket-creak that used to mean someone was drawing water for the morning tea.

He did not know about ships, or fold routes, or the particular kind of leaving that does not end in the next valley. He only knew: they had not come back, and a house that is not lived in starts, very slowly, to forget how.

---

They did come back. Not the ones who left — those were long since dust, or star-dust, depending which record you trusted — but their children's children's children, three of them, stepping carefully over the threshold of a house they had only ever seen in a single faded photograph passed hand to hand across five transits and one fold crossing. They spoke the old language haltingly, the way you speak something you learned from recordings rather than a grandmother's mouth. They had brought, of all things, a jar — not the same jar, the seed jar had been planted out and eaten and its line kept going for six generations of harvests on three different worlds — but a jar all the same, filled with soil from wherever they'd come from, because someone in the family had insisted, and nobody could say exactly why it mattered, only that it did.

The domovyk had almost forgotten what it felt like, a step on that particular floorboard, the third one from the door, the one that always announced a visitor before the visitor announced themselves.

He did not appear to them. Domovyky rarely do, and this one had gone a long time without practice. But he swept the ash from the hearth one more time, for the first time in longer than the house could count, and when the eldest of the three knelt at the cold fireplace and said — uncertainly, in the old language, reading the words off a translation card because no one had taught her properly — *"We are home. We kept the door in our hearts even when we didn't have the house,"* the domovyk understood, finally, the thing he had spent the empty years failing to understand.

They had never stopped believing the house was there. That was the whole of it. A house does not need to be lived in to be kept. It only needs someone, somewhere, to still be the kind of family that keeps it.

He lit no fire. He moved no object. He is not, whatever the old stories say of his kind, a thing that reaches into the world and changes it. But the youngest of the three swore, afterward, that the room felt warmer than an empty hearth had any right to feel — and her grandmother, who did not believe in domovyky any more than she believed in luck, still found herself leaving out a small saucer of milk that night, the way her own grandmother once told her the old country used to do, for reasons she could not have explained to anyone who asked, and did not particularly want to.

*Cultural note, Survey Archive general collection annotation: Kate Ukrainka's Tales from the Old Hearth is the best-known work of nostalgic fantasy read across the Earth-diaspora communities of the Concordance — not only the Celtic Union, whose own devotional and cultural record this Archive holds more extensively (see [The Life of Saint Aoife](/star-rangers/codex/life-of-saint-aoife/), [Hymn of the Thin Places](/star-rangers/codex/hymn-of-the-thin-places/)), but several smaller diaspora chapters this Archive has not yet catalogued in comparable depth. Ukrainka has been open, in the handful of interviews the Archive holds copies of, that she has never seen Earth and does not expect to: her grandmother's grandmother left before the Currach Fleet's own departure window, on an earlier and considerably less mythologised transit, and what Ukrainka writes from is inherited longing rather than memory — a distinction she has said she finds more interesting to write about than either homesickness or homecoming on their own. The domovyk, the rusalka, and the other household and field spirits that recur through her collected work are presented consistently as folk belief carried across the transit generations, not as confirmed entities; nothing in her author's notes, or in this Archive's holdings, treats them as anything other than what the family in this story treats them as — a way of describing what a kept house feels like, to people for whom keeping it was never a small thing.*
