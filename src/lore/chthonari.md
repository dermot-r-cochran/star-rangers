Ask a Chthonari engineer how they know a fold scaffold's re-sync gradient is off before the instruments confirm it, and the honest answer is always the same: it sounds wrong. Not audibly. Through the hull, through the deck plating, through whatever solid structure happens to be underfoot. A Chthonari has been reading stress through rock since before their species had spaceflight, and a fold scaffold's gradient error, to a body built for that, is just one more thing the structure is trying to say.

The **Chthonari** are a sapient, insectoid people native to the **Undersong Belt**, a dense asteroid cluster in the Skarn System that no single gravity well was ever generous enough to call a planet. They evolved to move, build, and communicate in rock and vacuum rather than open air, and every distinctive thing about Chthonari culture — the engineering reputation, the mathematics, the music, even the sport — traces back to the same physical fact: a Chthonari does not hear the world. It feels it, through substrate-borne vibration conducted straight into a body that never had much use for airborne sound to begin with.

## Physical Characteristics

A Chthonari stands small by most humanoid measures — roughly forearm-to-torso length for an off-world visitor's frame of reference — six-limbed, exoskeletal, and built low and dense for a life spent gripping tunnel walls in negligible gravity rather than standing upright against a strong one. The exoskeleton tolerates brief unsuited vacuum exposure without injury, a trait that made early Federation contact teams assume, incorrectly, that Chthonari were a fully vacuum-native species requiring no life support at all; sustained work and reproduction both require a sealed, pressurized burrow, and a Chthonari denied one for too long sickens the same as anyone would.

What the exoskeleton actually carries is an array of vibration-sensing organs distributed along each limb and the lower carapace — the organ set most outside biologists compare, inexactly, to a combined inner ear and lateral line. A Chthonari standing on solid rock reads the structure's own resonant behavior continuously and without conscious effort, the same way a human's peripheral vision registers motion without being asked to look for it.

## The Undersong

Chthonari communication, art, and — by their own account — thought itself run on **the Undersong**: a shared vocabulary of substrate-transmitted vibration, felt rather than heard, built from the same physical layer their tunnel-sense already monitors constantly. A burrow settlement's structural members are tuned, deliberately, during excavation, so that the settlement itself becomes a resonating instrument its residents are never not partly listening to.

This is why Chthonari mathematics and Chthonari music were never separate disciplines the way outside observers initially assumed. A harmonic relationship between two frequencies and a numerical ratio between two integers are, to a species that perceives structural resonance directly, close enough to the same fact that Chthonari philosophy has debated for millennia whether the distinction is worth maintaining at all. Federation mathematicians who have studied under Chthonari tutors consistently report the same experience: concepts that took years of abstract training to internalize — resonance, phase relationships, node structure — are, for a Chthonari student, closer to native intuition than acquired skill.

## Engineering Aptitude and the Star Rangers

That same intuition is why Chthonari individuals are disproportionately represented in the Star Rangers' Engineering Corps, specifically in fold-scaffold construction and maintenance. A [higher-dimensional fold scaffold](/star-rangers/lore/lagrange-fold-points/) is, at the level that matters for this comparison, a structure under gradient stress that must be read and corrected before it fails — precisely the category of problem Chthonari physiology has been solving, in rock, for longer than their species has had a written language. A Chthonari engineer working a scaffold's re-sync gradient is not using a metaphor borrowed from tunnel work. They are doing the same kind of reading, on a structure that happens to be made of coherence rather than stone.

No Chthonari is assigned to the Engineering Corps, and no Federation compact requires it — the charter's insistence that Ranger service be individual and apolitical applies to Chthonari exactly as it applies to every other recruit. The overrepresentation is a career choice made by a great many individuals who happen to be unusually good at the work, not a species quota, and Chthonari recruiters are, by several accounts, tired of explaining the difference to visiting press.

## Low-Gravity Sport: the Long Skitter

Where the Undersong Belt's negligible gravity shapes Chthonari engineering, it also shaped Chthonari sport. **The Long Skitter** — a competitive tunnel-transit race run through a deliberately unpredictable burrow course, scored on speed, structural finesse, and how cleanly a competitor reads the course's own resonance to anticipate turns before they're visible — has spread, as a novelty spectator event, to several off-world venues with low-gravity arenas built to host it. Off-world audiences mostly come for the speed. Chthonari spectators watch for something else entirely: whether a given racer is actually listening to the tunnel, or just memorizing the course like everyone assumes an off-world competitor has to.

## Interspecies Notes

Chthonari tolerate standard humanoid life-support without difficulty and interact easily with other Federation species in shared facilities, but the Undersong itself does not translate. Outside observers can be taught to recognize it exists; none, so far, have been documented learning to perceive it the way a Chthonari does natively. Chthonari serving off-world describe this as the one persistent, low-grade loneliness of the posting — not unkindness from colleagues, just a shared structure nobody else in the room can feel.
