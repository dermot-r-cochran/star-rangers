Ask a Mnemari mediator how they can quote both sides of a two-century-old boundary dispute word for word, and the answer is never framed as a feat. It is framed as a liability they have learned, slowly, to carry without it crushing them.

The **Mnemari** are a sapient, humanoid people native to **Ilenne**, distinguished from every other catalogued Federation species by two linked traits: Mnemari biology sets no natural upper bound on lifespan, and Mnemari cognition does not naturally lose episodic memory to time the way every other known sapient mind does. Neither trait makes a Mnemari hard to kill. Both make a Mnemari hard to finish knowing.

## Indefinite, Not Immortal

"Indefinite lifespan" is the precise term Federation xenobiology insists on, and Mnemari institutions insist on it too, for the same reason: it draws a line their own culture depends on. A Mnemari body does not senesce — no accumulating cellular damage, no organ decline, no frailty that comes simply from continuing to exist. Left alone, a Mnemari does not die of old age, full stop, because there is no mechanism in their physiology that "old age" describes.

Left alone is doing real work in that sentence. Mnemari are exactly as vulnerable as anyone else to injury, disease, vacuum, weapons fire, and every other ordinary way a body stops functioning — Mnemari actuarial tables, unlike every other species' on record, are driven entirely by accident and violence, with a "natural causes" column that Federation demographers still find genuinely strange to look at. The oldest confirmed Mnemari, at last Federation census, was past her eleventh century and showed no biological indicator that she was closer to death than a Mnemari fifty years out of childhood. She was not, by her own account, the most remarkable person on Ilenne. She had simply been careful, and somewhat lucky, for a very long time.

## A Memory That Doesn't Fade

The trait Mnemari culture treats as the harder one to live with is not the lifespan. It's that nothing in a Mnemari mind erodes the way memory erodes in every other species surveyed. A Mnemari's recollection of an event from four hundred years back carries the same fidelity, the same sensory weight, as one from four days back — no natural fading, no soft-focus nostalgia doing anyone the mercy of rounding an old grief down to something bearable.

This is why Mnemari are disproportionately sought, across the Federation, as mediators, archivists, and treaty-witnesses: a Mnemari negotiator who sat at a table two centuries ago does not need the record pulled to know exactly what was promised and in what words. Federation assembly sessions dealing with old, contested compacts routinely request a Mnemari present specifically because a Mnemari's memory of the original terms cannot drift the way institutional record-keeping, translation, and self-interested retelling all tend to drift over enough centuries. It is a role built directly on the same problem the Star Rangers themselves keep running into elsewhere in the Solar System record: what happens to the truth of an event once everyone who could contradict a convenient retelling of it is gone. A Mnemari at the table is one fewer way for that to happen quietly.

It is also, by near-universal Mnemari account, the trait that costs the most. A Mnemari does not get to decide a wound is old. Grief, betrayal, and loss stay exactly as sharp as the day they happened, available in full whenever something calls them back up, for as long as the Mnemari who holds them keeps existing. Mnemari psychology has an entire clinical vocabulary, untranslatable in a single word, for the specific kind of exhaustion that comes not from carrying too many years but from carrying every one of them at full resolution, indefinitely.

## The Setting-Down

Mnemari civic life is organized around a custom outside observers usually need explained twice: **the Setting-Down**, a voluntary, formally witnessed retirement from active public and professional life, undertaken not at a fixed age — there being no fixed age that means anything to a Mnemari — but whenever an individual judges their own accumulated memory load is beginning to crowd out their capacity to act well in the present. A Mnemari who Sets Down does not stop existing, and does not lose what they carry. They step back from office, trade, and formal responsibility, and move into what Ilenne calls **the Held Roles** — contemplative, consultative work: tending archives, teaching the very young, sitting as a witness of last resort when a dispute needs a memory nobody can contest. It is not exile and it is not failure. Ilenne's own civic architecture treats the Setting-Down as evidence a person has been paying honest attention to themselves, and a Mnemari who refuses to Set Down long past the point their peers can see the strain is a recognized, quietly worrying figure in Mnemari society, not an admired one.

The custom does double duty that Federation political scientists note approvingly: without it, a species with no natural mortality curve would have no natural mechanism for institutional turnover at all, and Mnemari office-holders would simply never leave. With it, Ilenne's public offices turn over on roughly the same rhythm a mortal species' do — just for a different reason, chosen rather than imposed.

## Population and Kinship

Mnemari birthrate is low by deliberate cultural design, not biological necessity, on the reasoning that a species which does not lose its elders to time has no need to replace them at the pace a mortal species does. Bringing a new Mnemari mind into being is treated on Ilenne as a rare, weighty, multi-generational commitment rather than a private family matter, planned by kin-councils on timescales that would read as absurd to a species used to counting a generation in decades. A Mnemari child grows and matures at a pace outside observers find unremarkable; what differs is everything downstream of maturity, once "how much longer does this person have" stops being a question that means anything.

## Interspecies Notes

Mnemari are fully compatible with standard humanoid life-support and atmospheric standards and interact easily with other Federation species in shared facilities. They cannot interbreed with humans or any other known species. Off-world Mnemari, particularly those serving long postings among mortal-lifespan colleagues, describe a specific, recurring grief with no equivalent in most other species' emotional vocabulary: outliving, in full clarity, everyone who mattered to them in a single posting, over and over, for as long as they choose to keep serving. Most Federation institutions that station Mnemari long-term have learned, sometimes the hard way, to build a formal offboarding ritual around a Mnemari colleague's eventual departure or Setting-Down — not for the Mnemari's sake alone, but because the mortal colleagues who remain are the ones who won't get to remember it as clearly.
