Solar Command has occupied the same command floor on Mars for four centuries. The Monasteries of Mars have spent most of those same four centuries making sure they are the one Martian institution that floor has no channel to reach.

The **Monasteries of Mars** are a loose association of independent contemplative houses built into the open, thin-atmosphere terrain beyond Mars's domed settlements — pressure-hermitages rather than sealed communities, each self-sufficient, each governed entirely by its own resident order, and each committed to a founding principle stated plainly in every house's charter: withdrawal from institutional life is the practice, not a precondition for some other practice. There is no central house, no ruling abbot, and no interest in acquiring either.

## Not the Fellowship, and Not Interested in Becoming It

Off-world visitors reliably assume the Monasteries are a Martian chapter of the [Fellowship of Light](/star-rangers/lore/fellowship-of-light/), and are reliably corrected. The Fellowship's contemplative houses train observers to watch boundary places on the world's behalf, in service of a vocation that eventually produces something like the Star Rangers. The Monasteries of Mars watch nothing on anyone's behalf. Their withdrawal is not preparation for later engagement; it is the entire point, indefinitely extended.

Fellowship chapters have proposed affiliation more than once across four centuries, each proposal framed as recognition of shared practice. Each was declined, and Martian monastic record keeps the correspondence on file specifically so the question stops being asked in good faith and starts being answered by precedent instead. The two orders are not rivals — a Fellowship contemplative and a Martian hermit have, on the handful of documented occasions they've met, gotten along without difficulty — but they are not kin, either, and the Monasteries have never once described the resemblance as more than coincidence.

## Why Mars

The order's founders were, by their own surviving records, reacting to the same fact that shaped so much of Martian civic identity: every government the Solar System has produced chose to run its command floor from Martian soil, and every grievance the outer system held against that government therefore arrived, rhetorically, at Mars's own front door — regardless of whether any individual Martian had a hand in the policy being resented. The first hermitages were built by residents who wanted the maximum available distance from that condition without leaving the planet that had produced it, reasoning that the point needed making from inside rather than from somewhere safely elsewhere.

That reasoning produced a specific discipline rather than a vague retreat. Hermitage life is built around the same open, unterraformed ground that Martian civic culture has repeatedly voted to keep unfinished — thin atmosphere, extreme temperature swings, and total dependence on a resident's own self-sufficiency skill rather than dome infrastructure or outside supply. Endurance of that thinness, sustained deliberately rather than merely tolerated, is the order's closest thing to shared theology: a physically thin world chosen on purpose, over a metaphysically thin one that found the Fellowship instead.

## Structure and Contact

Each hermitage is fully autonomous, self-provisioning, and closed to unannounced visitors; the small minority that accept any visitors at all do so on a schedule set entirely by the house, not the visitor. Martian civic government maintains a standing non-interference policy toward hermitage land, the same hard line it draws with Solar Command's own authority on Mars, and for the same reason: both institutions have learned that respecting the boundary costs less than testing it.

No hermitage has ever sought, or been offered, any role in Solar System governance, boundary-zone operations, or the [Star Rangers'](/star-rangers/lore/formation-of-star-rangers/) work. That absence is not oversight. It is four centuries of consistent, documented refusal.
