Every automated survey classifier has a bin for signals it cannot parse: unmodulated, non-repeating, no known protocol match. File under noise. The Sentinel's pulse sat in that bin for centuries before anyone thought to just listen to it.

**The Sentinel** is a small, airless world sharing the Halyx System with [Meridian](/star-rangers/lore/planets/meridian/), close enough in-system that early Cerebraun survey logs treated the two as a matched pair before instrumentation confirmed the Sentinel was geologically inert and biologically empty. No atmosphere, no native life, no resource concentration worth a Hegemony extraction license. By every metric the [Cerebraun Hegemony](/star-rangers/lore/cerebraun-hegemony/) uses to prioritise a world, the Sentinel does not matter.

Except for the pulse.

## The Pulse

Something beneath the Sentinel's surface emits a continuous, precisely periodic electromagnetic signal, strong enough to register on any receiver in-system and consistent enough that early Hegemony survey teams catalogued it, reasonably, as an automated navigational hazard beacon — the kind of thing a long-dead or long-forgotten builder leaves running because turning it off was never anyone's job. It matched no known beacon protocol, encoded no retrievable data by any decoding method tried, and threatened nothing. Successive survey passes filed it, reclassified it, and moved on. That is what centuries of automated classification is built to do with a signal that fits no template: note it, bound it, ignore it.

## What Shepherd Heard

The record places the catch in [Tissadelle Shepherd](/star-rangers/characters/tissadelle-shepherd/)'s service between her Officer and Chief years — closer to routine archived-telemetry review than a command decision, which is part of why it stands out. Reviewing Hegemony survey data made available under a Survey Corps data-exchange arrangement, she did what her training with [Slipwave](/star-rangers/glossary/slipwave/) had taught her to do with any signal that didn't behave the way it was supposed to: she stopped reading it as data and ran it as audio.

Pattern-recognition software had correctly reported that the pulse matched no known beacon or communication protocol. It had not been asked whether the pulse had *structure* — repetition with variation, phrasing, something closer to a motif returning changed than a mechanism repeating identically. Shepherd, whose ear had spent two years compensating for pitch drift in unshielded dock common rooms, heard exactly that on the first pass. What generations of classifiers had filed as noise because it matched nothing, she flagged as something considerably harder to file: it sounded, unmistakably, like music.

## Reclassification

The finding forced the Sentinel's designation off "automated hazard beacon, low priority" and onto an active anomaly docket — the kind of reclassification that, elsewhere in Star Rangers institutional history, has taken far longer to happen than it should have. The Survey Corps has not yet determined origin, intent, or mechanism. Musical structure is not proof of intelligence behind it, and nobody working the case claims otherwise; it is proof only that the signal is not what everyone had been comfortable assuming it was.

Whether the Sentinel is Meridian-built, older than Meridian's own reef-cities, or unconnected to Meridian entirely remains unresolved. The parallel to Meridian sapients' own claw-percussion communication — audible, rhythmic, readable at range — has not gone unnoticed by the analysts working the case. Administrator-Voice Sohrel, who holds the Halyx System liaison portfolio and authorised the independent verification that got the file reopened at all, has, on the diplomatic record, declined three separate Hegemony requests to raise the question with Meridian's reef-cities directly.

[Season 3, Episode 1](/star-rangers/seasons/s03/e01/s03e01c01/) dramatises the night of the catch, in both Shepherd's point of view and Sohrel's.
