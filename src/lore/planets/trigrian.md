The first survey report filed from Trígrian's surface opens with a wildlife observation and a formal request for an investigation into who, exactly, had been seeding lions on an uncharted world. The investigation was closed within the season. Nobody had.

**Trígrian** is the second planet of the **Triúr System**, a hierarchical trinary: a tight central pair of orange suns circling each other close enough to share a sky, and a smaller, whiter companion on a wide outer orbit. The planet rides a stable circumbinary track around the inner pair, and for most of its long year the third sun keeps its distance on the far side of the sky. The name — from the old Irish for *three suns* — was entered in the register by a Celtic Union cartographer seconded to the survey, and stuck the way informal names do when the alternative is a catalogue number.

## The Triple Sunset

For a few tendays each orbit, Trígrian's geometry brings all three suns into the same quarter of the sky, and at dusk they go down together: the close pair side by side, the white companion trailing them to the horizon. Atmospheric dust off the dry plains stretches the event into a slow amber descent that survey crews have been known to schedule shore rotations around, and which the report's own author — in a document otherwise composed entirely in survey register — called *worth the transit on its own*.

The season of converging suns is also the planet's ecological metronome. Triple-sunset season is peak heat and peak drought; the grasslands cure to gold, the herd species mass toward the remaining water, and the predators follow. Most of what the survey learned about Trígrian's fauna, it learned in the weeks when the three suns set together and everything on the plain came to the same few rivers.

## A Lioness That Isn't

The observation that opened the first report remains the planet's calling card. Trígrian's apex grassland predator is, at any distance a field scope closes, a lioness: the build, the tawny coat, the low patient posture in cover, the social hunting in related groups. The resemblance was close enough that the survey lead — aware that Terran stock has turned up in strange places before, and that [SSDC's genetic archives](/star-rangers/lore/crisis-centuries-terran-wildlife/) have made big cats a maintained lineage rather than a lost one — treated deliberate seeding as the *conservative* hypothesis and filed accordingly.

The biochemistry closed the question the way it closed the botanist's on [Tír na nÓg](/star-rangers/lore/planets/tir-na-nog/): pigment chemistry first, then protein handedness, then a reproductive strategy nothing Terran shares — the "prides" are sororal colonies that share a single breeding matriarch, closer in structure to a mole-rat colony than to anything feline. Trígrian's lioness is convergence, not descent: the same grassland problem — big migratory prey, cover that rewards ambush, heat that punishes solitary pursuit — answered the same way twice, twelve hundred light-years apart. Survey crews call the species the **grianmhór** (the report's glossary translates it, unapologetically, as *big sun-cat*), and the entry has become the standard teaching example in Ranger xenobiology induction: resemblance is evidence of shared problems, not shared ancestry.

## Survey Status

Trígrian holds a standing multi-generational survey window under [Frontier Transformation Protocols](/star-rangers/lore/frontier-transformation-protocols/) — not because anyone has proposed transforming a world that already works, but because the protocols' sapience clause applies with unusual force to a biosphere this behaviourally sophisticated. The grianmhór colonies coordinate hunts across kilometre distances by means the survey has not yet fully characterised, and until it has, doctrine treats the question of Trígrian's ceiling as open. No settlement authorisation exists, none has been requested, and the only permanent human presence is a rotating survey station on the northern plateau, staffed lightest in triple-sunset season, when everyone finds a reason to be in the field.
