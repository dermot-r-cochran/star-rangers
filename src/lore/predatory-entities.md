Three classes of predatory entity in the Star Rangers universe exploit social, emotional, and moral dynamics to feed. They are sometimes called "beautiful monsters" because they typically manifest as, or mimic, attractive or admirable forms. None of them use force as their primary method; all of them rely on maintaining ambiguity.

## Court-Fae

Court-Fae are Conceptual↔Etheric predators. They feed on:
- Ambiguity (unresolved social situations)
- Desire loops (wanting something without pursuing or releasing it)
- Social debt (obligations that are never clearly defined or discharged)

They resist clarity because clarity destroys their feeding condition. They use passive aggression rather than confrontation. After extended contact they leave *residue*: lingering obsession, subtle value drift, a background hum of obligation that persists after the contact ends.

**How to counter them:** Name what you know. Make the ambiguous explicit. Discharge or consciously release social debts. Court-Fae cannot feed on a loop that has been broken.

*In the story:* The entity at the Marsh Causeway is operating with Court-Fae methods — it is feeding on the ambiguity of Elvira's failed rituals and on her incomplete understanding of why they keep failing.

## Gilded Saints

Gilded Saints are virtue-extractors. They feed on:
- Moral labor (the effort of being consistently ethical)
- Sanctified suffering (pain or sacrifice that is given spiritual significance)
- Closure-prevention (ensuring that resolution never quite arrives)

They do not appear as threats. They appear as worthy causes, inspiring figures, or sacred duties. They encourage you to give more than is necessary, to define your worth through effort and suffering, and to believe that you have not yet done enough.

**How to counter them:** Recognise the closure-prevention pattern. Accept that enough is enough. The absence of suffering is not abandonment of virtue.

## Cute Predators

Cute predators (the named examples are "tickle bugs" and "lamb-masks") operate at the micro-scale of consent. They feed on:
- Micro-consent collapses (the small "yes" you give because saying "no" feels rude)
- Normalised boundary erosion (the gradual process by which "stop" becomes socially difficult)
- Harmlessness framing (they present themselves as too small or too charming to be worth resisting)

They are the most difficult predators to name because their individual effects are genuinely small. The damage is cumulative and comes from the habit of not saying "no."

**How to counter them:** The robots and cyber-enhanced animals of the detective agency are notably good at detecting cute predators early — their detection of asymmetry is not distorted by social pressure to find small things charming.
