:::pov krast

Eleven weeks in, and the hearing chamber had stopped feeling like a chamber and started feeling like weather — something you dressed for rather than attended. Krast had given testimony to enough review boards across thirty years of service to recognise the particular fatigue of a room that had asked the same question in nine different phrasings and was preparing to ask it a tenth time.

The delegate from whichever outer station had the floor this session — Krast had stopped tracking which one, not from disrespect but from a simple ceiling on how much a person could hold in working memory across eleven weeks — had just finished reciting response-time figures for a supply emergency the MSC's committee structure had taken four months to authorise action on. Four months. Krast did not dispute the figure. She had reviewed the underlying file herself before testimony began, and it was accurate, and accuracy was not the same as refutation, whatever the delegate's tone implied it should be.

"General Krast," the hearing chair said, "the chair would ask you to respond directly, if you're able, to the pattern Delegate Solano has just described. Not the single incident. The pattern."

She was able. That was, in its way, the entire problem with the next several minutes: she was entirely able to answer honestly, and honesty was going to cost her something she had not yet finished calculating.

"I'll answer the pattern," Krast said. "The MSC's committee structure is slow. I'm not going to stand here across eleven weeks of testimony and tell this hearing that isn't true, because the record already shows it's true, and a general who argues with her own record in front of people who've read it is not defending the institution, she's insulting the hearing's intelligence." She let that land before continuing, because the room needed to hear that she was not here to dispute facts, only to dispute what the facts were supposed to mean. "What I will defend is why the structure is built slow, deliberately, rather than fast by accident. This body keeps convening this same hearing, generation after generation, as though speed were a value with no cost attached to it. I was a junior officer during the last four years of the Coherence Wars. I watched what a system optimised for speed and popular sentiment did with both, four decades running. Forty years. Three billion dead. Every one of those deaths came from institutions that could move fast — faster than the MSC ever has, faster than this hearing would ever authorise anything to move today."

"General—"

"I'm not finished, and I'd ask the chair's patience for one more sentence, because I think it's the only sentence in eleven weeks that's actually said what I believe rather than what I've been defending by inference." She had not planned the sentence in advance. She would spend a long time afterward being unable to decide whether that made it more honest or less forgivable. "We tried democracy. The result was forty years of the Coherence Wars and three billion dead. I would rather be called a tyrant than repeat the experiment."

The chamber did the thing chambers do when a sentence lands wrong for the room and right for the speaker: it went very quiet in a way that had nothing to do with agreement.

Krast did not, in that moment, feel she had said anything except what thirty years of service had actually taught her. She would go on believing that for a long time afterward, in the specific way people go on believing the thing that requires them not to examine too closely why nobody else in the room seemed to be believing it with her.

:::pov solano

Three of them had brought recording units into the gallery that morning, and none of the three had coordinated with the other two — Solano knew this for a fact because she'd have recognised the coordination, having spent four years learning to recognise exactly that kind of arrangement in Halyard Station's own consultative council, where every vote that mattered had already been arranged before the session that was supposed to decide it.

She had not brought hers to catch anything. She had brought it because Halyard's council had sent her to this hearing with one instruction — *come back with something the station can use* — and eleven weeks of technical testimony about response-time percentiles had produced nothing a station council, half of them working double shifts on a supply chain the MSC's last review had taken four months to approve fixing, would sit still long enough to hear.

Then General Krast answered the chair's question honestly, and Solano's thumb was already on the unit before she'd consciously decided anything.

*We tried democracy. The result was forty years of the Coherence Wars and three billion dead. I would rather be called a tyrant than repeat the experiment.*

She sat very still for exactly as long as it took to be sure she'd actually captured it clean, audio and framing both, and then she did the thing she would spend the rest of her life being asked to justify and never quite managing to regret: she did not wait for the hearing chair's permission, did not check whether release was customary, did not weigh whether four months of patient, substantive testimony about accountability gaps deserved to be remembered for anything other than a single quotable sentence a general hadn't meant to volunteer. She filed it for release to Halyard's own station feed within the hour, with a two-line note: *This is the sentence the hearing needed eleven weeks to produce. Everything else was true. This is the part that's memorable.*

She had some private unease about that even as she sent it — a fear, not fully examined at the time, that a soundbite would do the political work the actual findings deserved to do on their own merits, and that doing it this way taught every institution watching that the truth needed a viral sentence to matter rather than a sound argument. She filed it anyway. The findings had been sitting in front of outer-station delegations for six years, in Eden's case, without producing a single change in MSC conduct. She had run out of patience for waiting on the sound argument to do the work by itself.

Within two hours, two of the other recording units had released their own copies, unprompted, each convinced independently that they were the one who'd caught it. Within forty-eight, it was running on twenty-seven outer-station feeds, cut to nine seconds, captioned with nothing but the general's name, rank, and the word she hadn't meant to volunteer standing alone at the end of it.

Solano would read, months later, an MSC officer's private complaint that the quote had been "taken out of context." She did not think this was true, and had reread the full transcript twice specifically to check. Krast's context made the sentence worse, not better — a general who genuinely believed accountability could be safely deferred as long as competence was doing the deferring, said so plainly, in a hearing convened specifically to ask whether that belief still held. Solano had not distorted the general's meaning. She had simply been the first of three people willing to let the room hear it without a technical footnote softening the landing.

She filed her formal testimony two days later, citing response-time figures the hearing had already heard nine times from nine other delegates. Nobody outside the chamber ever asked her about it. Everybody, eventually, asked her about the nine seconds.
