:::pov solano

Four years since the hearing chamber, and Solano had stopped being surprised by how little the room's furniture changed between institutions that had supposedly replaced each other. Same long table. Same tiered gallery. A different crest on the wall and a different acronym on the letterhead, and otherwise the exact chamber the Consolidation Hearing had run six months too long in, now hosting the negotiation that hearing had actually been for.

She was not here to release anything this time. That distinction mattered to her more than she'd expected it to. Four years of sitting on delegations, reading draft language line by line, arguing measurement thresholds with people who wanted the threshold set exactly loose enough to keep discretion for themselves — none of it photographed well, none of it fit in nine seconds, and all of it was the actual work the nine seconds had only ever bought time for.

"Strike 'in exceptional circumstances,'" she said, for the third time that morning, to the same clause. "Every exception clause this body has ever written has become the paragraph the exception swallows the rule inside of. If the corps has no jurisdiction on planets or inside self-governing habitats, it has none. Not 'none, except.'"

Commodore Voss did not concede it gracefully, which she had come to respect about him more than she'd expected to. He argued the point on its merits, every time, rather than performing agreement he didn't feel and reversing it later in language nobody caught until implementation.

"Strike it and you get a corps that has to stand outside a burning habitat and ask permission to enter," Voss said. "I've stood in rooms where that delay cost lives, Delegate. I'm not inventing the scenario to win an argument. It happened at Kessler Anchorage, eleven years ago, under this command structure's own predecessor, and the report is in this hearing's own record."

"I've read the Kessler report. I've also read what this command structure's predecessor did with every 'exceptional circumstances' clause it was ever handed, going back to the founding of the MSC itself. You're asking me to trust that this specific version of the exception will be read narrowly, by officers none of us have met yet, in institutions not yet built, indefinitely, forever. I can't accept that trade. Not because I doubt your own reading of it. Because I know mine won't be the reading that matters once the ink's dry."

It was the same argument she'd been having, in different words, since the hearing chamber four years ago — the same argument, she was beginning to understand, every accountability structure eventually has with every competent person convinced their own good judgment should be the exception clause. She had not come to this table believing Voss was Krast. She left every session more convinced of it. That made the argument harder, not easier: a bad-faith opponent could be out-argued. A sincere one just kept coming back with a better version of the same reasonable-sounding exception, and the work of holding the line never got to declare itself finished.

"Then we write the corps a mandate broad enough to save the habitat and narrow enough that it can never be pointed at one," she said. "Humanitarian. Operational. No governance, no policing, no side in anyone's dispute. That's not a compromise between your version and mine, Commodore. That's the only version either of us actually wants, once you take the word 'exceptional' out of it."

Voss looked, for the first time all morning, like a man considering a sentence rather than rebutting one.

:::pov voss

Voss had spent thirty years believing that competence, properly credentialed and properly supervised, was the load-bearing wall of any institution worth trusting with other people's safety. He still believed it. What four years of Accords drafting and charter negotiation had done to him was narrower and more specific: he no longer believed competence was sufficient by itself, which was a smaller admission than it sounded like from outside his own head and a considerably larger one from inside it.

He had known Krast. Not closely — she had been three ranks above him for most of his service, and the distance between them had been exactly the distance rank was supposed to preserve. But he had been in the gallery, eleven weeks into a hearing he'd expected to end in three, when she said the sentence that outlived every finding either side produced. He had believed her, in the moment. He still, four years and one institutional collapse later, believed she had meant every word of it honestly. That was the specific fact he had never been able to explain to delegates like Solano in a way that satisfied either of them: Krast had not lied to that chamber. The chamber's own accountability structure — the one that let her go on believing herself correct for thirty years without anyone in a position to test it — was the actual defendant, and Krast had simply been the most articulate witness the prosecution could have asked for.

He came to this negotiation, still, as the officer arguing that stripping the new corps of jurisdiction over planets and self-governing habitats created real gaps — Kessler Anchorage was real, the elapsed hours were real, the two engineers who died waiting for an authorisation chain to clear were real, and no amount of delegate skepticism made that report less true. He had not stopped believing operational readiness mattered. What Solano's four years of unglamorous, unphotographed argument had done was convince him that the choice was never between readiness and restraint. It was between an institution that could justify anything to itself in an emergency, and one that had already decided, in writing, before any emergency arrived, exactly how far it was permitted to go.

That was, he was beginning to suspect, the actual lesson of the hearing chamber — not the nine seconds everyone else remembered, but the six years of unread findings that had produced it. An institution capable of justifying its own exceptions to itself, indefinitely, was not a safeguard. It was Krast's chamber, waiting for its own next honest witness.

He signed his section of the draft that afternoon without the exceptional-circumstances clause in it. He did not describe this to anyone, afterward, as a concession. He had come to believe, somewhere across four years of losing this exact argument to a woman who had never once raised her voice to win it, that it was the more competent position, not the less — a rare admission for an officer of his service record to make, and one he made anyway, because the alternative was continuing to be right about operations and wrong about everything the operations were supposed to be in service of.

The Star Rangers Charter was ratified the following morning. Voss watched Solano sign her delegation's copy without ceremony, the way a person signs something they have already done the actual work of deciding, and thought, not unkindly, that the room owed her considerably more credit than the nine seconds anyone would ever remember her for. She had never once asked for it. He suspected that was, in the end, most of the reason she'd earned it.
