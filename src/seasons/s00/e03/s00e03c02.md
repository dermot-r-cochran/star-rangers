:::pov pitch

She had not travelled off Eden in eleven years, and the reception hall at Solar Command felt, walking into it, like a room built for a version of her career that had happened to someone else. Which was, in the specific and literal sense the charter's own printed preamble now made true, almost accurate.

*Founding assets: the Constant Margin navigational data; Dr. Y. Pitch's declassified fold-mechanics analysis; eleven years of documented anomaly observation at Eden Space Habitat.*

Declassified. Not *supplementary*. Not *see also*. She had read the sentence four times on the transit over, and a fifth time now, standing in a hall with better lighting than any committee room had ever given her testimony, and she still could not locate the specific moment the word had changed, only that eleven years and one disappearance and one six-month hearing and one signature stood between the two versions of that sentence, and none of those eleven years had been under her control at any point.

"You could look less like you're at your own funeral," said a voice she placed before she turned, mostly by the particular flatness that came from a person who had once told her the model made a thing *correct*, not *authorised*, and had never since needed either word explained back to her.

"Captain."

"Not anymore, technically. Survey Corps rank structure took my old title and made it mean something slightly different by committee, the way these things do." Kilbride did not look uncomfortable in the dress uniform, which surprised Pitch less than it might have eleven years ago. "You should be proud of the sentence. I read it twice on my own way in."

"I keep thinking about the other sentence. The one in the same document, four lines down." She did not have to specify which. Kilbride's face told her she didn't need to. *The open questions from the Patience First recovery are part of the Survey Corps' founding research mandate.* Six people, one document, one clause that committed an institution to keep asking a question forever rather than answer it once. "I built a model that got you there and back. I never built anything that could have told Okafor whether it was safe to go further. I still haven't. Eleven years, and I still haven't."

"No," Kilbride said. "You built a model that was honest about exactly how far it went. That's not the same as failing to build one that went further. I watched you say that difference out loud to her before she left. She heard you. She went anyway, with her eyes open, the same as I did with mine two years before her." A pause, unhurried, the kind Pitch remembered from a docking ring eleven years gone. "The founding mandate isn't a monument to a mistake, Doctor. It's an institution that exists specifically so the next person who goes further than anyone's tested has somewhere to report back to, if they can, and somewhere that keeps looking, if they can't. That's not nothing. It's considerably more than a footnote ever did for anybody."

:::pov kilbride

Survey Corps recruitment had approached her eight months into the Consolidation Accords with a question she'd expected to spend the rest of her working life never being asked by any official body: would she be willing to teach the corridor transit to people who'd be doing it under a charter instead of under nobody's authority at all.

She had said yes before the recruiter finished the sentence, which the recruiter had noted, on the record, as unusually fast for a decision that reassigned an entire career. Kilbride had not experienced it as fast. She had experienced it as eleven years overdue — an institution finally catching up to a decision she and two other people had already made, alone, on an unauthorised manifest, before anyone in a position of authority had a framework ready to receive it.

Standing now in a reception hall thick with officials she'd have had no reason to meet under the old MSC command structure, Kilbride found she was less interested in the charter's language than in who it hadn't managed to bring home. Six names had gone out on *Patience First*. One document, now ratified, kept an official, permanent line open for all six, worded carefully enough that it would neither promise a recovery nor foreclose one. She had read that clause the way she suspected every founding Survey Corps recruit eventually would: not as policy, but as an institution's way of saying *we have not stopped looking*, in the only language an institution actually has for saying it.

She had flown with Renner and Dez on *Constant Margin*. She had trained with Okafor for four months before *Patience First* launched, back when "trained with" still meant something as simple as two pilots comparing notes over a shared instrument log, before either of their names carried the weight the charter's founding document now quietly assigned them. Okafor had asked her once, in that instrument bay, whether the corridor got easier the second time. Kilbride had told her the truth: it got easier to fly and harder to trust, because the first time you don't know enough to be afraid of the right things, and the second time you do.

She raised whatever was in her glass, once, privately, to a name the charter didn't print and the reception wouldn't miss her doing it for, and then went to find Dr. Pitch again, because the woman had built the only honest map anyone had ever gotten before flying somewhere new, and an institution that had finally learned to cite that honestly instead of burying it was, whatever else the evening's ceremony amounted to, worth the drink.
