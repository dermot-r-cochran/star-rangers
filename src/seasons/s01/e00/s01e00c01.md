:::pov aldera

Aldera had misjudged a leap two nights before and still favoured the paw for it, which suited her. A limp was a good excuse to refuse being lifted, stroked, or carried indoors before she was finished with a room.

She kept three paces behind Elvira on the terrace walk, then six ahead, matching nothing. Matching nothing was the only reliable way to test whether anyone was paying attention. No one was, except the household guards in the high windows, and they were paid to watch Elvira, not the cat.

At the sundial, Elvira did the thing she always did: stopped mid-stride, tilted her head, exhaled slowly through pursed lips, as if bracing for a blow that hadn't landed yet. Aldera had catalogued the gesture for months. It happened at the sundial. It happened at the east wall. It had happened twice at the old well and nowhere else on the grounds. She had no name for what triggered it. She only knew it was real, and that Elvira treated it as nothing.

Today Aldera closed the distance and said so, the only way available to her — three quick strides, claws out, a flat warning yowl.

Elvira knelt, caught her by the scruff, and held her face up close enough to smell the sugar on her breath. "There's nothing here. Nothing coming. Go and amuse yourself." A swipe, not hard enough to draw blood — a correction, not an attack — earned her a sharp exhale and a wave toward the footman.

He carried her to the garden gate the way he carried everything: efficiently, without opinion. He set her down outside the latch and went back in. Aldera stood at the bars for one breath, maybe two, watching Elvira's skirts recede up the terrace path.

Then she turned the other way.

The city below smelled like nothing on the terrace ever did — tanneries, spice stalls, wet rope. She kept low, avoided the scarlet-coated household guards who hated stray cats on principle, and let instinct rather than memory carry her down toward the harbour. By the time she reached the wall her hop had a limp built into it that would not heal for weeks. She did not slow down.

She found the first ship that smelled of stale bread instead of cargo, and went in low, under a slop bucket in the galley. The cook found her once, aimed a kick that missed on purpose, and announced to no one in particular that they kept rats, not cats. She left the door ajar afterward. Tolerated, not wanted. It was enough.

When the ship groaned off its moorings, Aldera felt Dún Rí come loose from her the way a knot comes loose — slowly, then all at once. She crouched at the prow as the shore lights blurred to a gold smear and then to nothing. No wall. No hedge. No hand to claim her.

She licked the split pad of her paw. The pain was clean in a way none of the terrace's comforts had ever been.

:::pov elvira

Elvira noticed the cat was gone before anyone told her, in the specific way she noticed most things — a held-breath in the household's rhythm, a chore not reported as done. She did not call for a search immediately. She had learned, from the Fellowship at the old river abbey, that overreacting to a small absence taught the household nothing except that she could be provoked.

She had gone to that abbey twice this year already, drawn by a restlessness she'd stopped pretending was ordinary. Brother Daire kept a forge there that produced no metalwork anyone could sell, and a locked wing of the library where the shelving ran to vellum instead of paper. *Your sensitivity runs in your mother's line,* he had told her, tapping a diagram that seemed to hold its shape wrong when she looked at it directly. *The boundaries are thin here, and thinning further. You are not mad. You are reading something real.* He had a household name for it — the Fellowship called it the Overfold — and Elvira had taken the word without asking what stood behind it, because a name, even an imprecise one, was better than the alternative: standing in a garden at dusk feeling watched by nothing she could point to.

She did not yet know the word was a folk label for something the Survey Corps would eventually classify with instruments instead of intuition. She only knew the metallic taste in the autumn air was not imagination, and that the cat had always reacted to the same moments she did — the sundial, the east wall, the well — a half-second before she did.

By full dark, the cat had not come back for her evening feeding, which had never once failed to summon her. Elvira stood at the garden gate holding the untouched dish and understood, with a coldness that had nothing to do with the season, that this was not the cat sulking somewhere warm. Something had actually changed.

She set the dish down anyway. Then she went inside to find the household sage, because if the animals really did know first, she intended to find out what this one now knew that she did not.
