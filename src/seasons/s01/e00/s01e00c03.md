:::pov aldera

The crew put her ashore at first light. "We don't need bad luck," the mate said, and swung her off the deck without ceremony. Aldera landed hard on rounded stones, shook off the impact, and watched the hull's shadow sweep past and away. No instructions. No backward glance. The sails filled and the ship went on without her.

The island was knife-edge rock and drowned coves, salt grass and old ash from someone's abandoned camp. Hunger here was not the terrace's mild discomfort before a scheduled meal — it was a screaming, structural fact. She tried a tide pool first: water clear as glass, shrimp too fast to catch, salt that stung worse than it satisfied. She tried a dry fissure under a juniper: shells, bird droppings, no comfort and no memory of home.

She called out anyway, the way she would have called in the dark corridors of Dún Rí, expecting an answer from habit rather than reason. The sound died against the rock.

Something answered instead — a long, level growl, low as a struck string.

The shape that came out of the mist was built like Aldera but twice her length, pelt dappled the colour of old leaves, scarred pale in places by fights Aldera had no context for. Its eyes held a flat, uncanny amber that did not blink on the usual schedule. Aldera pressed into the shadow of the rock, unsure whether to challenge it or fold.

The larger cat sat, unhurried, and studied her the way Elvira studied a failed measurement — with attention, not alarm. Then it looked away and began grooming a foreleg with exaggerated calm. *No threat. Yet.*

They shared the tide pool in silence for a while. Aldera missed the shrimp twice and nearly soaked herself; the larger cat plunged a foreleg once, retrieved a crab, and pushed the remains toward her with a single paw. A test, not a gift. Aldera ate what was offered, and understood the difference.

By midday they had a working arrangement rather than a friendship: how to read the sand for buried eggs, how to feint a minnow into shallow water, where rain collected in a hollow stone that never quite dried out. Aldera failed more than she succeeded. She learned anyway.

It was at the rain-hollow, both of them lapping side by side, that the older cat finally spoke in a way Aldera could log rather than merely feel.

"You're carrying a wrongness with you," it said. Not unkindly. "Not from this island. From wherever you came from. I've met it once before, a long way south of here — a place where the ground disagrees with itself. There's someone at a marsh causeway, out past the river mouth, trying to hold a line alone. She isn't managing it. If you can feel what I think you can feel, she could use you before she knows to ask."

Aldera did not fully understand what she was being told, only that it mattered, and that the shape of it matched every unnamed moment on the terrace at Dún Rí — the sundial, the east wall, the well.

"You have a name," she said. It was not a question, the way Elvira's had never quite been a question either.

"Nessa, if you need one for the record." A pause, amused. "You're already keeping one. I can tell."

That night, with nothing to write on but the memory of the day, Aldera filed her first entry, in the form the habit would keep for the rest of her life.

**FIELD LOG — Aldera. No assignment. No agency of record.**

Subject: self. Status: fed, uninjured beyond the paw, considerably less lost than yesterday.

Subject: Nessa (wild, unaugmented, constraint-literate by observation alone). Recognised my sensitivity without explanation from me. Recommends travel south and west to a marsh causeway where a boundary is being held by one person, insufficiently.

Recommendation: go. Filing now because filing, apparently, is what I do.

**END LOG**

:::pov nessa

Nessa smelled the wrongness before she saw the kitten — faint, foreign, the particular unevenness she had not caught wind of since her own travelling years. She came down off the ridge already deciding what to do about it, which was, mostly, nothing. Watch first. Newly arrived things on Carraig Bán rarely lasted the week regardless of what anyone did for them.

This one lasted longer than expected. It missed the shrimp, missed the eggs, missed the whole first morning's worth of easy food a hungrier animal would have found by instinct alone — and kept trying anyway, in a manner Nessa recognised from exactly one place before: a stubbornness that outran the body's own sense, the kind that either got an animal killed early or got it somewhere worth arriving at.

She pushed the crab across the sand mostly to see what the kitten would do with being given something outright. It ate it without pretending not to need it, which told Nessa more than a season of careful observation would have. No performance in the taking. Whatever this small, over-scented stranger was, it hadn't yet learned to be ashamed of hunger, and Nessa found, somewhat against her own long habit, that she preferred it that way.

The teaching that followed was not generosity, whatever it looked like from the outside. Nessa had learned this stretch of coastline the slow way, over more winters than she cared to count exactly, and passing pieces of that on cost her nothing she wasn't already carrying. She watched the kitten fail at the sand-eggs four times before finding the trick of it, and thought, not for the first time this island, that failure repeated with attention was the only teaching method that had ever actually worked on her either.

The wrongness settled the question by the rain-hollow. Up close, lapping alongside it, the unevenness in the kitten's scent resolved into something Nessa had a name for only because she had gone looking for one, decades back, in a place south of here where the ground itself had disagreed about what it was. She had not managed that place. She had gone, found a single exhausted woman trying to hold something too large for one set of hands, done what a young and overconfident cat could do about it, and left within the season knowing the limit of what she was built for. She was older now. Whatever was still needed at that causeway, it was not going to be met by a cat whose hunting range had shrunk to this one stretch of rock.

But the kitten in front of her carried the same wrongness she remembered, undiluted and untrained, and carried it the way a young thing carries a gift it hasn't yet been told is heavy. Nessa was not sentimental about what she said next. She had learned, at that causeway, exactly what one insufficient pair of hands costs a boundary held alone, and she was not going to watch a second one go to waste on a rock nobody needed defending.

"You're carrying a wrongness with you," she said, and meant it as diagnosis, not accusation. She told the kitten what she could — south and west, past the river mouth, one person managing badly what should never have been managed alone — and left out the rest, the season she'd spent there herself and the reasons she'd left, because the kitten had not yet asked and Nessa had learned not to hand over more than a listener was ready to carry.

When it asked her name, she gave it plainly, and did not correct the kitten's evident assumption that a name was all there was to give. Some debts were better paid forward than explained.

She watched the small shape work its way off the rock the next morning, uncertain-footed and entirely certain of direction, and thought that whatever was waiting at that causeway had just been sent considerably more than it had any reason to expect from a bare tidal island that owed it nothing.
