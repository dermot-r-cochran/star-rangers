:::pov galahad

The dock smelled of ozone and old metal — the specific combination that meant *boundary proximity*, though Galahad had never heard anyone in the survey corps use that term. They called it Threshold Stink. He called it home.

Dock Seven was the furthest point out, which meant the instruments never quite agreed on the time. Two chronometers on the wall showed different readings — not by much, barely forty seconds, but you noticed it when you lived here long enough. The survey office had filed three incident reports about it. The station administrators had filed two responses explaining there was nothing to file reports about.

He adjusted his coat and watched the new arrivals come through the pressure seal.

The tall woman came first — pale, unhurried, moving with that particular quality of attention that he had learned to recognise over years of working the boundary posts. She wasn't scanning for threats. She was *reading* the space. Her eyes moved across the dock like a hand running over a page in the dark, feeling for the raised letters.

*Krenyi*, he thought, with certainty. He'd worked with three before. The pattern was unmistakeable once you'd seen it.

Behind her: a robot — bipedal, civilian chassis, older model by the joint-casings, but with sensor arrays that were emphatically not standard.

He checked the roster on his handheld. Three new transfers, all assigned to the boundary survey. He was apparently now their team lead.

This was going to be interesting, and not in the way his supervisor had promised.

:::

:::pov syra

The station was exactly as the approach data had described and entirely different from what the data had implied.

Syra had learned, in her century and a half of navigating human-built environments, that data described surfaces and implications described pressures. The data said: *a mid-grade orbital station, boundary-proximate, crew complement forty-seven, mixed species.* The implication said: *everyone here is waiting for something to go wrong, and they have all agreed not to say so.*

She could feel the Concordant edge through the station's hull — not as a sensation exactly, more as a faint discontinuity in her own coherence. A slight lag between intention and action. A breath held somewhere behind the physical.

She filed it away. It was not a threat. It was information.

The human in the survey coat was watching her. She had been watched before — it was a standard feature of arriving anywhere — but he was watching her in the specific way that meant *I have worked with your kind before and I am deciding whether to update my model or not.* She found this preferable to the other kinds of watching.

She noted the robot behind her in the reflection of the dock window. Rook-7's sensor arrays had been active since they cleared the pressure seal. Good. It was the correct response.

"You're the team lead," she said to the human. It was not a question.

:::
