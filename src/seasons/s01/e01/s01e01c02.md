:::pov galahad

The survey office occupied a corner of the station that nobody had bothered to give a window. Galahad suspected this was intentional — windows here showed the boundary shimmer on bad days, and the boundary shimmer had a documented tendency to make new arrivals ask difficult questions.

He spread the incident reports across the table. Eleven years of them.

"The official position," he said, "is that the chronometer discrepancy is a calibration issue that has proved difficult to resolve. The unofficial position—" He paused. "Is that we stopped trying to resolve it after Year Three, because every time we recalibrated, it came back within forty-eight hours."

Syra was reading the reports in sequence, which was not how most people read reports. Most people skipped to the conclusion. She started at the earliest entry and worked forward, occasionally going back to re-read something she had already read.

Rook-7 had not sat down. It was standing near the door with its sensor arrays pointed at the wall — specifically, Galahad realised, at the section of wall that backed onto the boundary-proximate section of the station.

"Eleven years," Syra said, without looking up. "The first report predates the last major boundary survey by three years."

"Yes."

"There is no note in the record connecting those two facts."

"No. There isn't." He paused. "I noticed that too."

She looked up then, and something in her expression shifted — not warmth exactly, but a recalibration. A slight upgrade in her assessment of him. He found, to his mild surprise, that this mattered to him.

:::pov syra

The human was competent. More than that: he had noticed the gap in the record and sat with it rather than explaining it away. That was a rarer quality than it should have been.

Syra was building a model of the situation as she read. This was not analysis — it was more like listening to a room fill with sound, waiting for the point where the harmonics revealed the shape of the walls.

What the reports described, taken together, was a coherence leak. A point where the boundary between two Concordants had thinned, and the expressions of each were bleeding through into the other. The instruments could not agree on the time because the time was slightly different on each side of the boundary, and Threshold Station occupied both sides simultaneously.

This was not a malfunction. It was a condition.

She did not say this yet. The human was still in the part of the briefing where he was presenting facts and watching for reactions. She had learned to let people finish before offering interpretations they had not requested. It was more efficient and considerably more respectful.

Rook-7 was more interesting to her than the reports. Its sensor arrays were pointed at the wall with a specificity that suggested it had already identified something. It had been logging since they arrived — she had seen the status light.

She filed a note to herself: *ask Rook-7 what it has found. Not here. Later.*

The human's name was Thorne. She had not asked him to use a different form of address, and he had not offered one. This was, in her experience, a good beginning.
