:::pov galahad

The survey office occupied a corner of the station that nobody had bothered to give a window. Galahad had decided this was intentional. Windows on this side showed the boundary shimmer on bad days, and the shimmer had a documented tendency to make new arrivals ask difficult questions before they had the context to ask them well.

He spread the incident reports across the table. Eleven years of them.

"The official position," he said, "is that the chronometer discrepancy is a calibration issue that has proved difficult to resolve. The unofficial position—" He paused. "Is that we stopped trying to resolve it after Year Three, because every time we recalibrated, it came back within forty-eight hours."

Syra was reading the reports in sequence. Not how most people read reports — most people skipped to the conclusion. She started at the earliest entry and worked forward, occasionally returning to re-read something.

Rook-7 had not sat down. It stood near the door with its sensor arrays pointed at the wall — at the section of wall, Galahad realised, that backed onto the boundary-proximate section of the station.

"Eleven years," Syra said, without looking up. "The first report predates the last major boundary survey by three years."

"Yes."

"There is no note in the record connecting those two facts."

"No. There isn't." A pause. "I noticed that too."

She looked up then. Something in her expression shifted — not warmth exactly, but a recalibration. A slight upgrade in her assessment of him. He found, to his mild surprise, that this mattered to him.

:::pov syra

The human was competent. More than that: he had noticed the gap in the record and held it rather than explaining it away. That was a rarer quality than it should have been.

Syra was building a model of the situation as she read. Not analysis — more like listening to a room fill with sound, waiting for the point where the harmonics revealed the shape of the walls.

What the reports described, taken together, was a coherence leak. A point where the boundary between two [Concordants](/star-rangers/glossary/concordant/) had thinned, and the expressions of each were bleeding through into the other. The instruments could not agree on the time because the time was slightly different on each side of the boundary, and Threshold Station occupied both sides simultaneously.

This was not a malfunction. It was a condition.

She did not say this yet. The human was still in the part of the briefing where he presented facts and watched for reactions. She had learned to let people finish before offering interpretations they had not yet requested. More efficient. Considerably more respectful.

Rook-7 interested her more than the reports did. Its sensor arrays were aimed at the wall with a specificity that meant it had already identified something. The status light had been active since they arrived.

She made a note: *ask Rook-7 what it has found. Not here. Later.*

The human's name was Thorne. She had not asked him to use a different form of address, and he had not offered one. In her experience, this was a good beginning.
