:::pov galahad

The ritual had already failed by the time Galahad arrived. He could tell by the smell — a sharpness in the air, like something had been folded the wrong way. He had smelled it twice before. Both times at the site of a boundary event that someone had tried to manage with incomplete information.

The woman at the centre of the collapsed circle was sitting in the mud. This seemed practical. She was not weeping or staring blankly. She was *calculating*.

"It didn't work," she said, when he got close enough.

"I can see that." He crouched to examine the circle. The markers were all in place. The chalk work was careful, precise — the kind of careful that meant someone had been taught by someone good. The problem was not the technique.

"I drew it correctly," she said.

"I believe you." He traced one chalk line with his eyes, not touching it. "The ground moved. Not physically. The layer underneath — the pattern substrate. It shifted between when you drew this and when you tried to activate it."

She looked at him for the first time. *Where did you learn that language*, her expression said.

He chose not to answer the implied question. "What were you trying to do?"

"Close a door." A pause. "There is something coming through."

He stood up. "Yes. We know." He looked toward the ruins of the old causeway — a structure that predated every survey he had ever read, that did not appear correctly on any map he had used. "We are trying to understand what."

:::pov elvira

The survey man knew more than his coat implied.

Elvira had been working the boundary zone for three years by herself, since the previous outpost occupant had left under circumstances she had never been told and had stopped asking about. In three years she had developed a vocabulary for what she observed here. Most people she met either did not share that vocabulary or actively rejected it.

This one used it naturally.

She stood up, brushing mud from her knees. The failed circle was annoying rather than frightening — she would need to take new measurements before trying again, and whatever was pressing through the boundary had used the attempt to *learn* the pattern, which meant her next attempt would have to be different.

She was not afraid. She did not experience deep fear, which she had once thought was a character flaw and had since understood as a structural feature. It made the boundary work possible in ways she could not have managed otherwise.

"You're with the station survey," she said. Not a question. She had watched the station lights on the horizon for weeks, noted when they changed in ways that suggested new personnel.

"Yes."

"There's something else here," she said. She had been trying to decide how to say this for days. "Not the boundary drift. Something that *wants* the drift to continue." A pause. "I do not have a good name for it. The folk around here call it a Court thing. I think they are identifying its method correctly but not its nature."

The man was quiet for a moment. Behind him, from somewhere in the reeds, she heard a sound she had learned to associate with Aldera.

The cat, she suspected, had been following her for longer than she had been paying attention.
