:::pov galahad

He had been to the grove once before, seven years ago, on his first posting to a boundary-proximate station. His supervisor had pointed it out from the edge of the reeds and said: *don't go in there at night, and if you go in the day, don't touch the pale ones.* He had filed this under the long category of field advice that nobody would put in writing.

He was going in at night.

Aldera had found the connection in the quiet way the cat found everything: not by announcing it, but by being at the relevant place before anyone else and waiting. She had been sitting at the tree line when they arrived. The report she transmitted was twelve lines of precise observation and one line of conclusion. The conclusion: *the fault line runs under the grove. The grove is the anchor. The entity has not been drifting in through the boundary. It has been here.*

It had been here for a long time.

He stopped at the edge of the trees and looked up. Seven of them — large, old, bark the colour of old bone in the lamplight. The canopy moved above him. Not with the wind. The wind had died when they entered the clearing.

*Against the wind.* Phase-lag. He had read about it once, in a survey appendix nobody had indexed properly. He wished now he had read it more carefully.

"How long?" he said quietly.

Elvira was standing beside him. She had come immediately when he sent for her — no hesitation, no questions, just: *I'll be there.* She was looking at the trees the way she looked at everything: with the attention of someone who had been observing something for a long time and was finally seeing the mechanism.

"The oldest report I have for this grove is two hundred and forty years," she said. "But the causeway was built around it. They left a gap." A pause. "You do not build a causeway around seven trees unless someone told you not to touch them."

He looked at the gap in the old stonework. She was right. Someone had known.

Whatever had used those three centuries to get comfortable was still here. The question was what they were going to do about it.

:::pov elvira

She had been wrong about the entity. Not entirely — the Court-Fae identification of its *method* had been accurate, and Aldera's earlier observation about the feeding pattern was correct. But she had believed it was drifting in through the boundary, re-entering after each failed ritual. She had been calibrating for an incursion event.

It was not an incursion event. It had never left.

She stood among the pale trees and let herself understand this properly. Three hundred years was not three years. This was not a predator that had found an opening. This was a predator that had put down roots — not metaphorically, but through the trees, through the Etheric fault line, through a structure so old that the people who built the causeway had known to give it space.

The leaf phase-lag was constant. She had been in the grove before, on three occasions, and noticed it each time and classified it incorrectly. She had written in her notes: *unusual wind behaviour, possible topographic effect.* She had told herself this was satisfactory.

It was not satisfactory.

She had been wrong for three years. She could stop being wrong starting now.

"It is using the trees," she said, partly to Thorne and partly to herself, in the way she sometimes processed observations aloud. "The root structures. They go down into the layer below the physical. That is where it is sitting. Not at the boundary edge. At the root."

She looked at the largest tree — the one at the centre of the arc, older than the others by perhaps a century, its bark so pale it was almost luminescent. The grove's anchor. The entity's anchor.

"The rituals were never going to close it," she said. "I was trying to seal a door it was not using." A pause. "I have been helping it for three years. Every failed attempt, every loop of confusion — I was feeding it and trying to close it at the same time."

She said this without self-recrimination, because self-recrimination was not useful here. She said it clearly, because clarity was the only instrument that had ever been effective against this kind of entity.

Thorne did not say *you weren't to know* or *it wasn't your fault.* She appreciated this. Both statements would have been sentimental rather than accurate.

"What do we need to do?" he said instead.

She looked at the oldest tree.

"Interrupt the coupling," she said. "Not the boundary. The root."
