:::pov galahad

The leak alarm started as a stutter in the overhead strip lights and became a full station alert before Thorne reached the transit ring.

Section G had sealed correctly, but one maintenance tech was still on the wrong side of the inner door and losing air.
There was a service corridor route, but it would take six minutes.
Rook-7's estimate for useful consciousness on the trapped side was under three.

Dr. Iona Vale was already at the ring console when he arrived, reading the corridor feed with the stillness of someone doing arithmetic that could kill people if done badly.

"Can we move him?" Thorne asked.

"Only if I can keep visual lock," she said. "I need line of sight from this ring to his endpoint. No blind placement."

She pulled one camera feed, rejected it, then switched to a manual optical channel through an emergency inspection port.
Her jaw tightened.

"I have a lane now. Narrow and unstable. If the particulate plume crosses that lane, lock fails."

Thorne looked at the power board. Transit draw request was already climbing.
"How much does this cost us?"

"Enough that life support notices if you ask me to do it twice."

He keyed the authorisation anyway.

:::

:::pov iona

Iona hated that people called it teleportation as if the word made it simple.

What she had was not a miracle and not speed.
It was a brief coherence agreement between two points in space, maintained by power and precision and the fact that she could *see* where she was placing a breathing human body.

"Maintain position," she told the trapped tech over comms. "Do not turn your head. Do not move your feet."

She drove the harmonic pulse.

Every time, the first sensation landed the same way: a white spike behind the eyes, a forced full-body convulsion of signal and muscle memory.
The comparison in the clinical files was electroconvulsive therapy.
The comparison was accurate.

The tech appeared inside the ring hard enough to collapse to one knee but conscious, coughing, alive.

Iona sat down because her own hands were shaking.
Not fear. Neurological recoil.

"Post-jump checks," she said through clenched teeth. "Now. Him first, then me."

By protocol she logged both exposures.
By experience she added the warning she always added: repeated short-window jumps pushed anxiety scores up, mood stability down, and central fatigue through the floor.

Nobody liked that warning.
It stayed true anyway.

:::
