:::pov syra

Syra read the incident report twice before speaking.
The numbers were clean and unpleasant.

"This is the same system as corridor transit," she said, tapping the harmonic trace. "Smaller distance, same law. You do not get to call it different because it happens inside one station."

Thorne nodded, exhausted and attentive.
Dr. Vale sat opposite him with a cup she had not touched.

"Then we write the rule so nobody can pretend they misunderstood it later," Thorne said.

Syra approved of this sentence.

"Write this," she said. "No visual endpoint, no jump. No power margin, no jump. No post-jump medical clearance, no repeat jump."

Nobody objected.

:::

:::pov iona

Iona projected the clinical graph and watched the room go quiet.

"These are not edge-case complaints," she said. "They are expected outcomes under repeated exposure. Anxiety elevation. Depressive symptom growth. Central fatigue accumulation."

She did not soften the wording.
Soft wording made people gamble with other people's nervous systems.

"And yes," she added when she saw the question in Thorne's face, "the experience feels like electrical seizure therapy for most operators. Brief, survivable, and expensive to the brain."

Elvira was standing by the viewport with the same intent stillness she carried in the grove.
"So teleportation is a rescue knife, not a road," she said.

"Exactly," Iona said.
"A knife. Useful. Dangerous. Never casual."

She signed the protocol block and pushed it across the table.

:::

:::pov elvira

Elvira liked clear instruments.
The old rituals had failed partly because they had not been clear.

This was clear.

No mystical exception. No hidden mercy.
If they could see the endpoint, if the harmonics held, if the station could afford the power, a body could move.
If any part failed, they did not move the body and call the failure fate.

She read the protocol and added one line in her own hand:

*Record the cost as carefully as the success.*

Dr. Vale looked at the line, then at Elvira, and gave one precise nod.

"Approved," Thorne said.

Outside, station traffic continued on its ordinary clocks, while inside the room they finished writing the rule that would keep those clocks from hiding the price again.

:::
