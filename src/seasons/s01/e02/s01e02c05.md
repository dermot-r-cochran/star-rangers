:::pov syra

Syra read the incident report twice before she said anything, which was, for her, already a concession to how much the numbers deserved a first pass in silence. They were clean. They were also unpleasant, and cleanliness had never once made a number kinder.

"This is the same system as corridor transit," she said, tapping the harmonic trace projected above the table. "Smaller distance. Same law. You do not get to call it a different kind of thing because it happened inside one station instead of between two."

Thorne, across the table, had the specific exhaustion of a man who had made exactly one correct decision under time pressure that morning and had not yet decided whether he trusted himself to make a second one calmly. "Then we write the rule," he said, "so nobody downstream of us gets to misunderstand it the way I nearly did."

"Write this," Syra said, already reaching for the stylus before he had finished agreeing with himself. "No visual endpoint, no jump. No power margin, no jump. No post-jump medical clearance, no repeat jump."

"All three, every time?" Thorne said. "Even when the margin's thin and the alternative is watching someone die on the wrong side of a door for six minutes?"

"Especially then," Syra said. "That is exactly the condition under which someone convinces themselves the rule was written for easier days than the one they're standing in."

:::pov iona

Iona had been waiting for him to say something like that, and was almost grateful he'd said it before she had to go looking for the argument herself.

She projected the clinical graph rather than answer him directly, and let the room read it before she spoke over it. "These are not edge-case complaints," she said. "They are expected outcomes under repeated exposure. Anxiety elevation. Depressive symptom growth. Central fatigue accumulation." Every word landed exactly as blunt as she'd written it. Blunting it for comfort was how people ended up gambling with somebody else's nervous system and calling it initiative.

"I'm not arguing the risk isn't real," Thorne said. "I watched it happen to Petrenko two hours ago. I'm asking whether a rule with no exception clause actually protects anyone — or just protects whoever wrote it from ever having to make the call twice."

Something in that landed closer to home than he'd probably meant it to. Iona set the stylus down.

"Say the rest of that sentence," she said. "The one you're not finishing."

"If I'd had ninety seconds less lock margin this morning, I'd have jumped him anyway. I know I would have. I want to know whether this protocol is honest about that, or whether it's a rule everyone in this room is already quietly planning to break the next time it's inconvenient."

Nobody answered that immediately, which Iona noted was its own kind of answer.

"Then that's the thing you should be afraid of," she said finally, "not the rule itself. A rule people quietly plan to break the moment it costs them something isn't a safeguard. It's paperwork with a due date on it. You write the rule to hold on the day it's hardest to keep, not the day it's easy — because the easy day was never the one that needed it."

Elvira, over by the viewport, had said nothing yet. She had the same intent stillness Iona remembered from the grove — the look of someone listening for the shape underneath an argument rather than the argument itself.

"You're both describing the same choice from two ends," Elvira said. "He wants a rule good enough to trust in the moment it's hardest. You want a rule that doesn't quietly go optional the day it's hardest. Those aren't opposite requirements. That's one requirement, said twice."

Iona looked at her for a moment, then allowed herself something close to a smile. "Say it back to him properly, then. I don't think he heard it from me."

"No exception clause," Elvira said, to Thorne directly, "because you already told us you'd have broken one. The rule isn't there to stop a good officer in a genuine crisis. It's there so a good officer in a genuine crisis has to feel the whole weight of breaking it — instead of signing a form that quietly excuses him in advance."

Thorne was quiet long enough that Iona wondered whether he'd take the point badly. He didn't.

"That's—" he started, and stopped, and started again. "That's a harder rule than the one I was arguing for. I still think it's the right one."

"So do I," Iona said. "That's usually how you can tell."

:::pov elvira

Elvira liked instruments that told the truth about themselves. Her old rituals at the causeway had failed, in part, because they never had — a circle could look complete and still be lying about what it actually held, and she had spent three years learning to feel the difference by touch before she ever had a word for it.

This protocol did not lie about itself. No visual endpoint, no jump. No power margin, no jump. No clearance, no repeat. No mystical exception tucked into a subclause for the day someone senior enough needed one. If they could see the endpoint, if the harmonics held, if the station could afford the draw, a body could move. If any one of those failed, they did not move the body and call the failure fate — the way the folk around the causeway called every unexplained loss a Court thing and left it there, unexamined, because examining it cost more than the loss itself seemed to.

She had watched Thorne argue himself toward the harder version of the rule rather than the easier one, which she recognized, because she had done the same thing herself in the grove three nights ago, out loud, in front of a man she had only just started trusting with the truth. It cost something to choose the harder rule on purpose. She respected people who paid it anyway.

She read the finished protocol through once, then took up a stylus that felt unfamiliar in a hand more used to chalk, and added one line in her own script beneath the last clause:

*Record the cost as carefully as the success.*

It was the same instruction she would have given her own working, if she'd had the sense to give it to herself three years ago, before every failed circle taught her the same lesson the expensive way.

Dr. Vale read the line, looked up at her, and gave one precise nod — the nod, Elvira was beginning to recognize, of someone who did not hand out agreement casually, and therefore meant it fully when she did.

"Approved," Thorne said.

Outside the viewport, station traffic went on keeping its ordinary clocks, unaware that the room behind the glass had just finished writing the rule that would keep those clocks from ever hiding the price again.
