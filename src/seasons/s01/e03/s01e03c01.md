:::pov galahad

The alarms on Pelagos Minor had a calm voice.
That was the first thing Galahad noticed when the Basin sirens began.
Not screaming. Not panic tones. A clear directive repeated every twelve seconds over every public channel:

*Priority boarding order: women. Then children. Then older adults. Hold your lane. Compliance preserves life.*

He had heard harsher systems fail because they sounded like fear.
This one sounded like procedure.
People listened.

The sky over the basin had split into three colours by then — storm-green in the upper layer, iron-grey under it, and a low seam of copper where the atmospheric fault was cutting through the launch shields.
Each shuttle lift looked temporary, improvised against mathematics that no longer liked the planet.

They moved people by category and scan-tag.
No exceptions.

At first the policy looked like mercy made efficient.
Women were boarded fast.
Children in bundled clusters.
Older people carried, wheeled, lifted, shouted forward through lanes that actually held.

Galahad spent six hours in Corridor C keeping order where order kept people alive.
He watched men in their twenties and thirties pass children over their shoulders, pass mothers their place marks, pass water, pass air-filters, pass any argument about fairness because arguing would slow the line and slowing the line would kill everyone.

When Corridor C collapsed, they rerouted.
When Corridor B lost pressure, they rerouted again.
When Corridor A failed, there was nowhere left to route.

The final transport rose half full because the docking arm sheared before they could seal the aft ramp.
He could still see the men running toward it as it lifted.
Not screaming.
Just running, as if effort could revise physics.

Afterward, in the temporary shelters above the floodline, people called the evacuation a success because thirty-eight thousand lived.
Galahad signed the same report.
He used the word *success*.
His hand shook while he wrote it.

Success had a shape.
The shelters had queues for medicine, queues for food, queues for grief counselling, and in every queue there were too many widows under forty and too many boys with no uncles left to answer the questions that come after sirens stop.

No one had designed this.
Everyone had obeyed.
The result was still real.

:::pov syra

Syra requested the raw manifests before she requested sleep.

Policy language interested her less than outcomes did.
Words were intent-signals.
Manifests were structure.

She projected the departure logs across the command table and let the room go quiet.
Rows of names. Age bands. Assigned priority. Boarding timestamps. Missing tags.

"The protocol did what it was written to do," one local administrator said, voice brittle with defensiveness and exhaustion.
"It maximized immediate survivability."

"Yes," Syra said.
She did not soften agreement when agreement was true.
"And it also concentrated mortality into one demographic band."

She expanded the graph.
The missing were not random.
They formed a clean cliff in the population curve: young adult men.

Not because they were less brave.
Not because they failed to comply.
Because the policy always had one more category before them, until there were no departures left.

"You are implying fault," the administrator said.

"I am naming mechanism," Syra replied.
"Fault and mechanism are not identical questions."

Silence.
Then she continued, still precise, still unwilling to let grief become abstraction.

"A strictly applied safety order can preserve life in the first window and destabilize life in the second."
She pointed to the downstream models.
"Care burden increases. Partner loss increases. Birth-rate assumptions fail. Labour replacement lags. Informal violence risk rises where social structure has been abruptly thinned."

One councillor sat down as if gravity had changed.

"We followed the rule," he said.

"I know," Syra said.
"That is the tragedy."

She marked the file for Threshold Station under policy-triage, not misconduct.
Not absolution. Not accusation.
A record that later teams could use without pretending this had been either malice or fate.

:::

:::pov iona

By the third day, Iona stopped asking people who they had lost.
The question presumed a singular noun.
Most answers were lists.

The clinic tents had no space for philosophy, only bodies and nervous systems.
She treated crush injuries, smoke lungs, sleep collapse, dehydration.
Then the quieter injuries started arriving: panic spikes at boarding tones, dissociation when someone heard the words *priority order*, children who refused to leave doorways because leaving had become synonymous with never seeing someone again.

A woman in her thirties sat on the edge of a cot and said, very calmly, "My son keeps asking when his father and brothers are on the next shuttle."

There had been no next shuttle.

Iona wrote referral after referral, knowing the counsellors were already beyond capacity.
The surviving older men moved like people carrying twice their own weight.
The surviving young women moved like people who had been handed an impossible arithmetic and told to call it tomorrow.

At night, command asked for metrics.
They wanted injury totals, infection projections, medicine burn rates.
Necessary numbers.

Iona gave them all of that.
Then she added one field of her own:

**Secondary impact: demographic trauma following strict priority compliance.**

One officer asked her to rephrase it.
Something less charged.
Something operational.

She looked at him until he looked away.

"It *is* operational," she said.
"If we only measure the people who made the shuttle, we will write this policy again and call the same wound unavoidable."

On the sixth day, she stood at the edge of the basin with Galahad and Syra.
The water had settled into a false calm over what had been neighbourhoods.

"Did the policy save lives?" Galahad asked quietly.

"Yes," Iona said.

"Did it break this place?" he asked.

She watched a boy teaching his younger sister how to tie a thermal blanket into a sling because no adult hands were free.

"Also yes," she said.

No one spoke after that.
The answer did not become easier because it was accurate.
