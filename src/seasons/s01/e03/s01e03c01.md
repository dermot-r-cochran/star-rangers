:::pov galahad

The alarms on Pelagos Minor had a calm voice, and that was the first thing Galahad noticed when the Basin sirens began — not screaming, not the rising, jagged tone he'd braced for, but a clear directive repeated every twelve seconds over every public channel, level as a metronome: *Priority boarding order: women. Then children. Then older adults. Hold your lane. Compliance preserves life.*

He had heard harsher systems fail because they sounded like fear. This one sounded like procedure, and people listened to it the way people listen to something that has already decided not to lie to them.

The sky over the basin had split into three colours by the time he reached Corridor C: storm-green in the upper layer, iron-grey beneath it, and a low seam of copper where the atmospheric fault was cutting straight through the launch shields. Each shuttle lift looked temporary against mathematics that no longer liked the planet — improvised, insufficient, and going up anyway because insufficient was still better than nothing.

They moved people by category and scan-tag. No exceptions, and for the first several hours no visible cost either. Women boarded fast, in ordered files that held their shape even as the ground shook. Children went up in bundled clusters, sorted by a triage team that had clearly drilled for exactly this and never quite believed they'd need it. Older people were carried, wheeled, lifted, shouted forward through lanes that actually held their order under pressure that should have broken any queue Galahad had ever supervised.

He spent six hours in Corridor C keeping that order, because in Corridor C, order kept people alive. He watched men in their twenties and thirties pass children over their own shoulders to strangers, pass their place-marks to mothers who hadn't asked for them, pass water and air-filters hand to hand down a line, pass every argument they might have had about fairness — because arguing would slow the line, and slowing the line would kill everyone in it, including whoever was arguing.

When Corridor C collapsed, they rerouted. When Corridor B lost pressure an hour later, they rerouted again. When Corridor A failed close to the fourteenth hour, there was nowhere left to route to.

The final transport rose only half full, because the docking arm sheared before the crew could seal the aft ramp. Galahad watched the men who'd been queued for it running toward the ramp as it lifted anyway — not screaming, just running, the particular silent effort of people who seem to believe, for exactly as long as their legs are still moving, that effort alone might revise the physics happening above them. It didn't. He made himself keep watching until the transport cleared the shield line, because looking away first felt like a second abandonment layered on top of the one already happening, and he was not going to be the one who added that.

Afterward, in the temporary shelters above the floodline, people called the evacuation a success, because thirty-eight thousand people had lived through fourteen hours that should have killed most of them. Galahad signed the same report and used the same word. His hand was not steady when he wrote it, and he noticed that it wasn't, and he wrote the word anyway, because it was still the accurate one.

Success had a shape, once you stayed long enough to see it fully. The shelters ran queues for medicine, queues for food, queues for grief counselling — and in every one of those queues, Galahad started noticing the same pattern before anyone gave it a name: too many widows under forty, too many boys with no uncles left to answer the questions that come after the sirens finally stop. Nobody had designed this. Everybody had simply obeyed, in order, exactly as instructed, all the way to the end of the instructions. The result was no less real for having been nobody's intention.

:::pov syra

Syra requested the raw manifests before she requested sleep, because policy language interested her considerably less than outcomes did. Words were intent signals. Manifests were structure, and structure did not have the luxury of meaning to hide behind.

She projected the departure logs across the command table and let the room go quiet around them: rows of names, age bands, assigned priority, boarding timestamps, and — the column she kept returning to — missing tags.

"The protocol did what it was written to do," one local administrator said, her voice brittle with defensiveness worn thin by exhaustion. "It maximized immediate survivability."

"Yes," Syra said, plainly, the way she agreed with anything true regardless of who found it inconvenient to hear. "And it also concentrated mortality into one demographic band."

She expanded the graph until the missing resolved into a shape nobody in the room could keep pretending was random: a clean cliff in the population curve, cut precisely along the line of young adult men. Not because they were less brave. Not because they had failed to comply — if anything, they had complied more completely than any other cohort, passing their own place in line to someone else's child without being asked twice. Because the policy always had one more category scheduled ahead of them, right up until there were no more departures left to have.

"You are implying fault," the administrator said.

"I am naming a mechanism," Syra said. "Fault and mechanism are not the same question, and this room will need both answered eventually, but not in the same sentence, or you will spend the next decade arguing about the wrong one."

Silence held for a moment. Then she continued, unwilling to let the room's exhaustion soften into abstraction before the numbers had finished making their point.

"A strictly applied safety order can preserve life in the first window and destabilize life in the second," she said, and moved to the downstream models. "Care burden increases. Partner loss increases. Birth-rate assumptions fail. Labour replacement lags behind demand. Where a social structure is abruptly thinned this precisely, informal violence risk rises to fill the gap left behind."

One councillor sat down heavily, as though gravity itself had changed weight in the room.

"We followed the rule," he said, quietly, not as a defense so much as a question he didn't yet know how to finish asking.

"I know," Syra said. "That is the tragedy. Not that the rule was broken. That it was kept, perfectly, by everyone, all the way to an outcome none of you were looking at when you wrote it."

She marked the file for Threshold Station under policy-triage rather than misconduct — not an absolution, and not an accusation either, but a record precise enough that whichever team eventually inherited this problem would not be able to pretend it had been either malice or fate. It had been neither. It had been arithmetic nobody watched closely enough, running exactly as instructed, for fourteen hours, until it produced a number with a shape no one intended and everyone had, in their own small compliant way, helped build.

:::pov iona

By the third day, Iona had stopped asking people who they had lost. The question presumed a singular noun, and almost every answer she got back was a list.

The clinic tents had no room left for philosophy, only bodies and the nervous systems attached to them. She treated crush injuries first, then smoke lungs, then sleep collapse and dehydration, working the obvious damage before the quieter kind had even finished arriving. The quieter kind arrived anyway, on its own schedule: panic spikes triggered by the boarding tones replaying in someone's memory uninvited, dissociation at the mere sound of the words *priority order*, children who would not leave a doorway because leaving, in the last four days of their short lives, had come to mean never seeing someone again.

A woman in her thirties sat on the edge of a cot one evening and said, very calmly, in the voice of someone who had already run out of the version of this sentence that broke down crying, "My son keeps asking when his father and brothers are on the next shuttle."

There had been no next shuttle. There would not be one now.

Iona wrote referral after referral, knowing full well the counsellors on this rotation were already past capacity and would be for months. The surviving older men moved through the camp like people carrying twice their own weight in something nobody could see. The surviving young women moved like people who had been handed an impossible arithmetic problem and told, gently, that the answer was due tomorrow regardless of whether they'd finished it.

At night, command wanted metrics — injury totals, infection projections, medicine burn rates. Necessary numbers, and she gave them all of it without complaint. Then she added one field of her own, typed slowly enough that she couldn't later claim she hadn't meant every word of it:

**Secondary impact: demographic trauma following strict priority compliance.**

One officer asked her, not unkindly, to rephrase it as something less charged. Something more operational.

"It *is* operational," she said, and looked at him steadily enough that he was the one who looked away first. "If we only measure the people who made it onto a shuttle, we will write this exact policy again someday, somewhere else, and call the wound it leaves behind unavoidable. It was not unavoidable. It was simply not measured."

On the sixth day, she stood at the edge of the basin with Galahad and Syra, watching the water settle into a false calm over what had been, four days ago, somebody's neighbourhood.

"Did the policy save lives?" Galahad asked, quietly, in the tone of a man who already knew the answer and needed to hear it said aloud anyway.

"Yes," Iona said.

"Did it break this place?"

She watched a boy nearby teaching his younger sister how to tie a thermal blanket into a sling, because there were no adult hands free to do it for either of them.

"Also yes," she said.

Nobody spoke again for a while. The answer did not get any easier to hold simply because it happened to be accurate.
