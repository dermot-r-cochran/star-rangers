:::pov larsen

The referral had been sitting in Eden's queue for six days before anyone thought to call it interesting, and even then, nobody thought to call Larsen. He heard about it because he made a point of hearing about everything that sat in a queue for six days.

Detective Inspector Wendell Albercombe met him at the bureau's side door rather than the front desk, which told Larsen most of what he needed to know before either of them said a word. A man who wanted an audience walked you through the lobby. A man who wanted this over with met you by the stairs.

"It's a ration-manifest discrepancy," Albercombe said, handing over a data slate like it had personally inconvenienced him. "Eleven crates, logged out of Eden stores, never logged in anywhere. I'd have closed it as spoilage and moved on, except spoilage doesn't usually route itself through four transfer stamps and come back missing a fifth."

"Four transfer stamps."

"Four transfer stamps I can read. There's a fifth somewhere I can't, because it's not Eden's paperwork anymore once it leaves Eden's dock." Albercombe said this in the tone of a man reciting a complaint he'd already made twice to people who couldn't do anything about it. "Superintendent Oyelaran's read is that it's somebody else's problem the moment it crosses the boundary. I agree with her. I just also don't know whose."

That was the whole shape of it, and Larsen had heard the shape often enough to recognize it without needing the details spelled out further: a thing that started inside one habitat's tidy jurisdiction and ended somewhere the tidiness didn't reach. Compact courier manifests didn't lose five stamps by accident. They lost them because somebody along the route had a reason to make the sixth one illegible, and reasons like that rarely stayed contained to a single habitat's paperwork.

"You did the right thing sitting on it," Larsen said, which was as close as he ever got to thanking someone for admitting a case had outgrown them. "This is what we're for."

Albercombe's expression suggested he'd heard that line before and remained unconvinced it made the paperwork easier. He didn't argue it, though. He handed over the case file, the transfer logs, and — with the specific reluctance of a man surrendering the last thing he'd hoped to keep — a thermos.

"Tea," he said. "Not for you. For whoever's waiting for you outside. He gets tetchy if the visit runs long and nobody's fed him anything."

Larsen had not, as far as he knew, brought anyone with him. He said as much.

"You will have," Albercombe said, already walking back toward his desk, "by the time you're done reading that file properly."

He was, it turned out, entirely right — though it wasn't a person waiting by the door when Larsen came out reading the fifth transfer stamp's absence for the third time. It was a domestic-model intelligence, addressed by nobody as anything but Jeeves, standing exactly where a man might stand if he'd learned, over some years, precisely how long a case review took and had timed his arrival to the minute.

"The Inspector asked me to see you found the lift," Jeeves said, in the tone of a man delivering the least significant fact in the room. "He also mentioned you'd want to know that the fifth stamp isn't missing so much as reassigned — the manifest software doesn't delete a routing code, it just stops displaying one it's told isn't relevant anymore. Eden's own systems wouldn't know to look. I understand the Compact's do."

Larsen looked at the transfer log again. Then at Jeeves. Then, because it seemed only fair, at the thermos.

"He tell you to say that, or did you work it out yourself?"

"He told me to see you found the lift," Jeeves said. "I understood the rest to be implied."

Five habitats, one line, and apparently one very well-informed household intelligence who wasn't on anybody's roster and had no business knowing what a Compact-wide routing code looked like from underneath. Larsen decided, on the strength of eleven missing crates and a stranger's unreasonably good tea, that this case had already earned itself a second look.

:::pov petra-voss

The call came through Calloway's office before it reached Voss directly, which was the correct order and also the one she trusted least — by the time anything crossed Calloway's desk, it had already been smoothed into a shape convenient for four other people's schedules. She read past the smoothing anyway. She'd had two years of practice.

*Orbital Five-O has opened an inquiry into a cross-habitat supply discrepancy originating at Eden. Estimated scope: undetermined, pending Larsen's assessment.* Calloway's note beneath it was shorter and more honest than the summary above it: *He wouldn't have opened it if it weren't worth opening. You know how he is about paperwork he doesn't need.*

She did know. It was, in fact, the entire reason she'd hired him.

Voss had spent two years arguing to anyone who'd sit still for it that the Compact's five habitats needed one investigative line that answered to nobody's local convenience — not because the habitats couldn't police themselves, but because nothing built to police one habitat could ever be expected to notice a pattern that only existed across all five. She had commissioned Larsen on that exact theory, over the quiet objections of three Commissioners who considered a Governor's personal task force an insult to their own competence, and she had not once, in two years, regretted overruling them.

She regretted it slightly less this morning, reading a report that began at Eden and had already, before lunch, stopped being Eden's problem.

A supply discrepancy that could be explained by one habitat's sloppy accounting stayed exactly that — a filing matter, beneath a Governor's notice by design. A discrepancy that required someone to make a routing code deliberately unreadable was not sloppy. It was maintained, by someone with the standing to reach into a courier manifest and keep it that way, and Voss had governed the Compact long enough to know that "someone with that standing" was a very short list, none of whom would enjoy Larsen's attention.

She drafted no formal response. Larsen didn't need one; he needed the space to keep pulling the thread without four Commissioners deciding, individually and simultaneously, that it had wandered into their jurisdiction and required a committee. That was the entire point of Orbital Five-O, and the entire reason she'd built it to answer to her and no one slower.

She wrote back a single line, mostly for Calloway's benefit, since he was the one who'd have to field the calls: *Let him work it. Tell the Commissioners I said so.*

Then she sat with the report a moment longer than she needed to, because five habitats was a great many places for eleven crates to have gone missing from, and she had learned, the same two years she'd learned everything else about this job, that the ones that mattered never announced themselves as more than a filing matter until it was already too late to have caught them earlier.
