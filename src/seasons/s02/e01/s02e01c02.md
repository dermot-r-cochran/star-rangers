:::pov larsen

Calloway's office ran on the same principle as everything else about the man: nothing in it existed to impress a visitor. No window dressing for a Governor's task force commander walking in unannounced, no rearranged desk, no folder pulled conspicuously to the top of a stack to suggest urgency that hadn't been there five minutes earlier. Just a man who read paperwork for a living, doing exactly that, and glancing up at the door with the mild attentiveness of someone who'd expected the visit approximately when it arrived.

"You could have called ahead," Calloway said, which from him was not a complaint so much as an observation about a variable he liked to control.

"I did. Twenty minutes ago." Larsen set the routing code printout on the desk, reassigned digit circled twice in a hand that had given up pretending it wasn't annoyed about it. "You'll have gotten the notice about the time I was already in the lift."

"Efficient." Calloway didn't touch the printout yet. He looked at it the way he looked at every piece of paper that crossed his desk — as though it might be lying to him, and the only defense against that was reading it slowly enough to notice. "Eden's ration-manifest discrepancy. Eleven crates, a routing code somebody didn't want legible past the fourth stamp."

"You already knew the shape of it."

"I know the shape of everything that crosses this desk. That's the post." He finally picked up the printout, turned it a quarter turn, and went quiet in a way Larsen had learned, in two years of dealing with him, not to interrupt. Calloway's silences were work, not hesitation. "This courier contractor. Threnody Logistics. Cross-habitat freight, mostly bulk transport nobody looks at twice."

"You know them."

"I know their billing." Calloway set the printout down and, for the first time since Larsen had walked in, looked less like an administrator and more like a man revisiting a decision he'd made and was no longer entirely satisfied with. "Six weeks ago their quarterly reconciliation came through my office a hair's-breadth outside expected variance — not enough to flag as fraud, not enough to justify pulling four Commissioners into a meeting over a rounding discrepancy that resolved itself by the next filing cycle. I made a note. I did not escalate it. There was nothing yet to escalate."

"And now there's eleven crates."

"And now there's eleven crates that ran through the same contractor, during the same reconciliation window, with a routing code somebody knew exactly how to make illegible past a fourth stamp — which is not a mistake a courier company makes by accident, and not a level of manifest access a courier company usually has reason to want." Calloway said this without inflection, the way he said most things, but Larsen had spent enough years reading people who'd trained themselves out of visible reaction to hear what was underneath it: the specific, private frustration of a man whose entire post existed to catch exactly this kind of thing before it became someone else's emergency, discovering that he had caught it, six weeks ago, and filed it as a curiosity instead.

"You couldn't have known," Larsen said, mostly because it was true and partly because he suspected Calloway wouldn't say it to himself.

"I could have asked one more question about a rounding discrepancy that resolved itself suspiciously cleanly for something that was supposedly a mistake." Calloway slid the printout back across the desk, along with a second folder Larsen hadn't seen him retrieve. "Threnody Logistics' full account history with this office. Everything my desk has on them, going back three years. You didn't ask for it. I'm giving it to you anyway, because the alternative is waiting for you to request it formally through channels neither of us has time for, and because if I'm right about what a second look finds, I would like it on record that this office noticed before it was told to."

Larsen took the folder. "That's not administrative coordination. That's you doing my job."

"That's me reading my own inputs closely enough to have caught this the first time, which is the entire job, and which I failed to do." Calloway's tone hadn't changed at all, which was somehow worse than if it had. "Find out if I'm right, Commander. I'd rather know than keep filing it as a curiosity."

:::pov dorian-calloway

He had told Larsen the truth, which was easier than most people assumed once you'd built a career on the principle that a coordination office survives exactly as long as its inputs are honest ones. What he had not told Larsen — because it wasn't yet a fact, only a shape he didn't like the look of — was that Threnody Logistics' account had crossed his desk a second time since the reconciliation discrepancy, three weeks ago, attached to a routine cross-habitat freight authorization that Calloway had approved without a second read.

He pulled his own copy of that authorization now, alone, the way he did most of his actual thinking. Five habitats' worth of paperwork moved through this office in any given week, most of it exactly as dull as it looked, and the post only worked because Calloway had trained himself to trust the dullness until something specific told him not to. Six weeks ago, a rounding discrepancy that resolved itself. Three weeks ago, an authorization that had needed his signature and gotten it, because nothing about a freight manifest asks a Chief Commissioner to reread it twice.

He read it a second time now.

The authorization had been requested and countersigned at the Commissioner level — not Eden's, one of the other four, filed with a habitat seal Calloway recognized without needing to look it up, because recognizing every one of those seals on sight was, again, the entire job. It was not, on its face, anything but ordinary: cross-habitat freight authorizations crossed his desk daily, and a Commissioner countersigning one for a contractor already operating a legitimate route was not, by itself, a fact that meant anything at all.

It was, however, a fact that connected a name to a contractor Larsen was about to start pulling apart in earnest, and Calloway had spent two years building this office on the principle that he read his own inputs closely enough to catch the discrepancies before they became someone else's emergency. He had failed that principle once already this month.

He was not going to fail it twice by sitting on this the way he'd sat on the reconciliation notice — but he was also not going to hand Larsen a Commissioner's name on the strength of one countersigned authorization and a rounding error that had resolved itself too cleanly to be nothing. That was a conclusion, and Calloway's entire professional discipline ran on the difference between a conclusion and a fact that hadn't finished arguing for itself yet.

He made a second note instead, in the file he kept for exactly this kind of half-formed thing, and reached for the intercom to arrange a very ordinary, very unremarkable call to one particular Commissioner's office — the kind of call his post made a dozen times a week, and the kind that, this time, he intended to listen to rather more carefully than usual.
