:::pov dorian-calloway

He placed the call the way he placed every call to a habitat Commissioner: mid-morning, unscheduled, framed as the kind of routine liaison check his office made a dozen times a week, because a call that announced its own importance taught the person on the other end exactly how to prepare for it.

"Commissioner Duarte. Quarterly logistics review — nothing urgent, just closing out a few open lines before the reconciliation cycle locks." Calloway had said some version of this sentence perhaps four hundred times in two years. It had never once, before today, felt like it was doing actual work.

Anselm Duarte held Meridian's Commissioner post the way most of the five did — competently, without complaint, with the specific weariness of a man who'd spent a decade making sure his habitat's paperwork gave Calloway's office nothing to notice. "Always glad to close a line, Chief Commissioner. Threnody's contract's been quiet on our end. Reliable carrier. I don't think we've had cause to flag them in — three years, now, must be."

Three years was correct. Calloway had the number in front of him. It was also, he noted, a fact Duarte had produced without needing to check anything, for a contractor whose "quiet" three-year record Calloway's own reconciliation had just found a hairline crack in.

"That tracks with our records." It did. That was the trouble with the whole call, the same trouble Calloway had anticipated and dreaded in roughly equal measure: nothing Duarte said was false. Threnody's contract at Meridian was, on every figure Calloway could independently verify, exactly as quiet as Duarte described it. The freight authorization sat where it should sit, countersigned where it should be countersigned, timestamped inside a window that raised no flag on its own. A man reading only Meridian's side of this exchange would find nothing here worth a second look.

"Anything on your end I should know about?" Duarte asked, and the question was, on its face, the correct one for a Commissioner to ask when the Compact's coordination office called about a contractor operating in his own jurisdiction. Calloway had asked variations of it himself, to other Commissioners, more times than he could count. He listened to it now the way he'd learned to listen to everything that crossed this desk — not for what it said, but for what asking it this particular way, at this particular moment, cost the person asking.

It cost Duarte nothing visible. That, more than anything Duarte had actually said, was the detail Calloway carried out of the call with him.

"Nothing that needs your office yet," Calloway said, which was true in the narrowest technical sense and would stop being true within the hour, once he'd finished writing up exactly what "nothing yet" had actually contained. "Appreciate the time, Commissioner."

He ended the call and sat with it a moment, the way he sat with everything that didn't add up to a fact yet. Duarte had answered every question correctly, promptly, and with a specificity that a man improvising his answers rarely managed. That specificity was, itself, the thing Calloway's two years of reading inputs closely enough to catch the discrepancies had trained him to distrust: an answer that arrived too well-rehearsed for a question the caller had, by design, given no warning was coming.

He drafted the summary for Larsen in four sentences, deleted it, and wrote it again in three. There was no version of this that read as more than a hunch on paper, and Calloway had built his entire post on the discipline of not handing anyone a conclusion dressed up as a fact. He sent the three sentences anyway, because the alternative was sitting on a second curiosity the way he'd sat on the first one, and he had already told Larsen, out loud, that he wasn't going to do that twice.

:::pov larsen

Calloway's message was three sentences long and said, without quite saying it, that he'd just talked to someone who'd known exactly what to say before being asked.

Larsen had commanded a boarding team long enough to recognize the shape of that report even in someone else's careful phrasing. It wasn't evidence. Calloway hadn't claimed it was evidence, which was itself the most useful thing about the message — a man who'd spent two years training himself not to overstate a finding wasn't going to start now, and the fact that he'd sent anything at all, on three sentences of instinct, meant the instinct had cleared whatever bar Calloway set for himself before bothering a Governor's task force commander with it.

He read the message twice more, then pulled up what the Compact's own registry held on Meridian — not much, by the standard of a Governor's task force that had spent two years working almost exclusively out of Eden's jurisdiction. A habitat like any of the other four: self-governing, a Commissioner, a Mayor, a civil bureau running its own ordinary caseload, none of it built with an eye toward ever hosting a Five-O visit.

That was, in fact, the entire problem. Larsen's authority ran to all five habitats equally, on paper, and had been exercised at exactly one of them in two years, because the gap Five-O existed to close only opened when a case actually crossed a line — and until eleven crates and a reassigned routing code, nothing had. Going to Meridian in person wasn't a courtesy call. It was Five-O doing, for the first time since its founding, the one thing its charter had always technically allowed and its caseload had never yet required: showing up somewhere that hadn't asked for it, on the strength of a Chief Commissioner's hunch and a contractor's suspiciously clean paperwork.

He thought about Voss's line, delivered two years ago at his own commissioning and repeated often enough since that he no longer needed to look up the wording: *the limit is real because you enforce it, not because anyone's watching to see if you will.* Meridian was inside that limit. He wasn't crossing anything by going. He was, for the first time, actually using the whole of what the charter gave him.

He drafted the travel request, copied Calloway, and — after a moment's consideration that he did not examine too closely — copied the Governor's office as well, not because procedure required it at this stage, but because Voss had told him once that she'd rather hear about a Meridian trip from him than from Duarte's Mayor asking her why a Governor's task force had shown up unannounced.

*Going to Meridian myself,* he wrote. *Nothing formal yet. Want to see Duarte's face when I ask the second version of your question.*

He sent it before he could decide that was an unprofessional way to phrase a travel authorization, and found, checking the outbox a moment later, that he did not particularly regret it.
