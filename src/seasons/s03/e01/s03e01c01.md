:::pov tissadelle

The backlog queue did not care that Shepherd had made Principal eleven days ago. It was the same queue it had always been: raw telemetry from Hegemony survey passes, released to the Survey Corps a decade or two after the fact under the terms of an exchange agreement nobody on either side seemed to love, sorted by nothing more sophisticated than date received. Someone had to clear it. Nobody senior wanted to. That made it, by the unwritten logic of every institution Shepherd had ever served in, hers.

She had cleared four hundred and eleven files since second-shift start. The Sentinel's was file four hundred and twelve.

The classifier's note was three lines long: *unmodulated non-repeating EM source, no protocol match across current library, consistent periodicity, catalogued as automated hazard beacon, low priority, no further action recommended.* She had read variations of that note perhaps sixty times tonight alone. Her hand was already moving to confirm and advance when something made her stop — not the data, which she had barely looked at yet, but the phrase *non-repeating*. She had spent two years on a dock stage arguing with sound techs about exactly that word, and she had never once heard a signal that was actually non-repeating. Even feedback had a shape.

She pulled the raw waveform instead of the summary and did the thing she was fairly sure was not in her training record anywhere: she routed it to audio and put it through a headset instead of a spectrum display.

It took four seconds to know the classifier was wrong.

Not wrong that it matched no protocol — it didn't, she checked twice. Wrong that there was nothing there to match. What came through the headset had a pulse under the pulse, a phrase that returned eleven seconds later almost the same and not quite, the way a chorus comes back changed by whatever happened in the verse. She sat very still and listened to it four more times, the old habit surfacing without being asked for: catalogue the drift, find where it's compensating, find what it's compensating for.

It was not compensating for anything. It was doing it on purpose.

She logged the finding at 0340 ship's time, in language flat enough that she was fairly confident no one reading it cold would understand why her hands weren't quite steady: *Recommend reclassification. Signal exhibits structured repetition with variation inconsistent with mechanical origin or degraded beacon function. Requesting Hegemony liaison review before file is closed.*

Then she sat with the headset still on, replaying it once more for no operational reason at all, and thought about a rule on a hillside she wasn't supposed to think about on duty: *you don't go in past dusk, you don't ask why until you're ready to carry the answer.* She wasn't sure yet which side of that she was standing on. She filed the request anyway. That was the job — not being sure, and filing it regardless.

:::pov sohrel

The request arrived through the Ranger liaison channel at an hour Sohrel's central brain classified, correctly, as an hour no properly staffed human institution should have been generating paperwork at. *Reclassification: automated hazard beacon to unresolved anomaly. Grounds: structured audio pattern.* Sohrel read it twice, the second time slower, out of something that was not quite suspicion and not quite its opposite.

A junior officer of a neutral institution the Hegemony did not fund and did not command was asking the Prime Ganglion's own administration to reopen a file three centuries settled, on the strength of what amounted to: *I listened to it and it sounded like music.*

Sohrel's central brain drafted the correct response without difficulty. Politely worded. Procedurally sound. *The existing classification stands pending independent verification through established protocol; subjective audio impression is not, on its own, sufficient grounds for reclassification.* True. Defensible. The kind of answer that closed a file rather than reopening one, which was, on a world where every open file was a limb-ganglion's worth of attention the Prime Ganglion's structure did not have to spare, usually the correct answer.

The limb-ganglion governing Sohrel's third dorsal arm disagreed, and said so the only way it could: a slow color shift across four centimeters of skin, unreadable to any human on the relay station, that translated — approximately, the way anything cross-modal ever translated approximately — to *we catalogued that signal as a beacon three centuries ago because nobody wanted to be the one to say we didn't understand it, and we still don't, and now someone finally has.*

Sohrel did not override the limb. Sohrel never fully did; that was not how the negotiation worked, and pretending otherwise was the kind of category error the Hegemony accused everyone else of making. Sohrel sat with both positions — correct and unresolved — the way the species had always sat with such things, and drafted a second line beneath the first, one the human liaison would read as procedural caution and nothing more:

*Independent verification is authorised. Ranger officer's original telemetry log to be preserved unmodified pending review.*

It was not agreement. Sohrel was careful, even to itself, not to call it that. It was, at minimum, the Hegemony's version of not closing the door before checking who was on the other side of it — a courtesy Sohrel extended perhaps once a decade, and never, in three centuries of Administrator-Voice service, to a filing that had already been closed this long.

Three centuries was a great deal of noise to have been wrong about. Sohrel's third arm, humming faintly beneath the desk, seemed to think so too.
