:::pov tissadelle

The data cache changed hands at the council house in under four minutes. Tissadelle had expected longer — some ceremony to match the weight the Union's mediation council put on the standing agreement — but the clerk who received it treated the exchange exactly the way Threshold Station's own archivists treated any transfer: verify the seal, log the timestamp, sign for custody. She approved of that more than she expected to.

Everything after the council house was personal leave, logged as such, and she was careful about the distinction the way she was careful about most things. The Rangers had no jurisdiction on Tír na nÓg. They had an invitation, narrowly worded, covering one exchange of survey data on boundary-zone matters. Whatever she did once she was walking the uplands in her own coat instead of her service one, she did as her mother's daughter, not as a Line Captain. She had said this to herself twice on the transit down, which was usually a sign she didn't fully believe it yet.

The uplands smelled the way they always had — wet stone, cut gorse, the particular sweetness of the pale lowland flowers gone to seed. Her mother talked the whole climb, the way she always had, about the flock, about a cousin's dispute over grazing rights, about nothing that required an answer. Tissadelle listened with half her attention and catalogued the rest of it out of a habit four years deep: wind direction, ground moisture, the two ewes favoring their left forelegs.

She noticed the trees before she understood why she'd noticed them.

They stood in a shallow fold behind the home pasture, pale-barked, the leaves moving a half-beat behind the wind that moved everything else. She had read that exact description in a Survey Corps bulletin eighteen months ago, filed under Marsh Causeway flora, and had not thought about it since. She stopped walking.

"That's the Hollow," her mother said, not stopping. "You don't go in past dusk. You know that."

"I know the rule," Tissadelle said. "I didn't know why it was the rule."

Her mother looked back at her then, and Tissadelle understood — with the specific, unwelcome clarity of a hypothesis confirming itself — that she was no longer looking at this hillside the way she had left it. She was reading it. Bark chemistry, root geometry, a rule that had kept a family safe for longer than anyone here had a name for the reason. She could file this. She had the vocabulary, the classification, half a report already assembling itself in her head.

She also had no authority to write it. And, standing in her mother's back field with her mother's face waiting for an answer, she found she did not want the report. She wanted to tell her mother what the rule meant, plainly, as family — which was a different act entirely, and one the charter had never needed to forbid her, because it had never occurred to anyone drafting it that a Ranger might come home.

:::pov orla

Órla had known her daughter was different since the second year of training, when Tissadelle stopped saying *I think* and started saying *I haven't confirmed that yet* — a small change, but Órla had raised her and knew the shape of every sentence she owned. She had watched the girl's letters get shorter and more exact for four years and had told herself, mostly successfully, that exactness was not the same as distance.

Watching her stop dead at the edge of the Hollow told her otherwise.

The Hollow had rules older than the charter Tissadelle served under, older than the Union itself, carried down through six generations of women who had never once agreed on why the rules worked, only that they did. Leave the water clear. Don't cut the pale wood. Don't linger past dusk. Órla's own mother had called it respect. Órla, who had less patience for unexplained respect, had called it a habit worth keeping regardless, and left it there.

Her daughter was not going to leave it there. Órla could see the calculation running behind her eyes — the same look she used to bring home from cadet leave, back when she still apologized for it.

"You're not going to write this down," Órla said. It came out flatter than she meant it to, more like a boundary than a question.

"I'm not authorised to," Tissadelle said. "I have no standing here. None of this is Ranger ground."

"That's not what I asked."

Tissadelle was quiet long enough that Órla braced for the answer she'd been expecting since the girl first shipped out — some kindly, professional deflection, the kind strangers gave when they'd learned not to promise things. It didn't come.

"I'm not going to file it," her daughter said instead. "But I'm not going to leave you with just the rule, either. You should have the reason. You've earned the reason." A pause, the same pause she used to take before admitting she didn't know something, back before four years taught her to sound certain regardless. "It'll cost you the mystery. I don't know if that's a fair trade."

Órla thought about six generations of women keeping a rule they'd never been given the reason for, and about the particular, careful way her daughter had just offered to spend something official on something that was only theirs.

"Tell me walking," she said. "The light's going, and I'd still rather not be standing here past dusk — reason or no reason."

Her daughter almost smiled at that, the old smile, not the new careful one, and fell into step beside her. Órla listened to her explain roots and layers and a word — *Etheric* — that sat strangely in a voice that had once sung on a dock stage on Eden and thought: *there she is. Further off than I'd like. Still mine.*

:::pov galen

Galen had worked the Union gate long enough to stop expecting the handoff to look the same way twice, and the morning Line Captain Shepherd came through it still surprised him a little.

The Highland Guard detail brought her down from the uplands in good order and better silence — four of them, ceremonial rifles at rest, the kind of correctness that read less like hostility than like a people who had decided, generations back, that correctness was the safest shape for a feeling they didn't have a better container for. They said their farewells at the gate the way the charter specified: precisely, briefly, with no more warmth than the occasion strictly required and no less. Shepherd returned it in kind, which Galen had come to recognise as her own way of paying a courtesy back exactly as given, not a beat more or less.

She looked tired in the specific way leave sometimes left a Ranger tired — not the operational exhaustion Galen saw off every boundary-proximate rotation, but something quieter, like she'd spent four days doing arithmetic she hadn't been able to put down.

"Welcome back," Galen said, once the guard detail had turned and started back up the track.

Something in her shoulders came down half an inch. It was a small thing. Galen had learned to watch for small things; the gate rarely gave him anything larger to work with, and a small thing, read correctly, usually told him everything the paperwork wouldn't.

"It is good to home," she said, and caught it half a beat late, the correction arriving before he could have needed it. "I mean back home. With the Star Rangers."

Galen logged the manifest, confirmed the seal, and did not ask her what four days on her mother's hillside had cost her to be worth correcting that sentence for. Rangers who wanted to say something said it unprompted, in the gap between the guard leaving and the boarding call. He had learned, a long time ago, to leave that gap open and let her decide what went in it.

She didn't fill it further. He walked her to the aetoshuttle instead, logged the handover, and watched Union jurisdiction end the way it always did at this gate: quietly, on schedule, with nothing about the moment marked as significant except to the two people standing in it.

:::pov tissadelle

She had not expected the word to come out wrong. She had rehearsed nothing — there had been nothing to rehearse, only a hillside, a mother, and four days of noticing things she had no authority to write down — but somewhere between the guard detail's correct, chilly farewell and Galen's two ordinary words, the sentence had assembled itself before she'd finished choosing it.

*It is good to home.* Tír na nÓg was the only planet she had ever been born on. It was not, she understood the instant the words left her, the planet the sentence had been about.

She corrected it aloud, cleanly, the way she corrected any inaccurate report before it left her desk. Galen didn't ask why the first version had needed correcting, which she was grateful for in a way she didn't examine too closely. The Highland Guard's stiff, precise goodbye behind her had been exactly what the charter required and not one degree warmer — her mother's world extending her the correctness owed a visiting officer, nothing owed a daughter, because officially there was no daughter standing at this gate, only a Line Captain whose personal leave had ended on schedule.

She had not realised, until the guard turned and the tension went out of her shoulders on its own, how much of the last four days she had spent holding a posture for people who loved her and didn't quite know what to do with what she'd become. Galen didn't require the posture. Galen required a manifest, a seal, and nothing else, and that was, this morning, the most restful transaction she'd had since she stepped off the shuttle four days ago.

She thought of the Hollow once more before she boarded — the pale bark, the half-beat-late leaves, her mother's face asking her not to write it down — and filed the whole of it, deliberately, under leave rather than under record. Then she climbed the ramp into the aetoshuttle, and did not look back at the uplands the way she once, years ago, had been unable to stop herself from doing.
