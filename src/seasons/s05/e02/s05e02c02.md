:::pov tissadelle

The bulletin was routine in the specific way that made routine things dangerous to read quickly: forty pages of cross-referenced anomaly filings, compiled by an archivist whose unsigned annotations she had learned to recognise the shape of years before she ever learned the name behind them. Sen's notes did not editorialise. They simply put two things next to each other that nobody else had thought to put next to each other, and let the adjacency do the arguing.

Item fourteen was the Marsh Causeway grove, cross-referenced against her own service record from a season she rarely thought about anymore. *Dryadic-type Etheric anchor. Leaf phase-lag, sub-second, consistent across seven specimens.* She had read that description exactly once before, in a briefing that had cost her nothing to sit through.

Item sixteen cost her considerably more.

*Union land-survey entry, filed under general flora anomaly, never flagged for follow-up. Leaf phase-lag consistent with item fourteen's marker.* A coordinate string. Nothing else — no name, no croft, no six generations of women keeping a rule they'd never been given the reason for. Just numbers, the way every unimportant thing in an archive is, eventually, just numbers.

She had the coordinates converted before she'd finished deciding whether she wanted to know.

Boirinn Uplands. Close enough to the Shepherd holding that "close enough" was doing no real work as a hedge.

She sat with the bulletin open in front of her for four seconds — the exact length of time, she noted with the part of her mind that never fully turned off, that it had taken her to know the Sentinel's pulse wasn't noise, a lifetime and one rank ago. Then she did the thing that was, she was fairly sure, the only version of this she could live with: she flagged item sixteen for formal survey priority, cited the phase-lag consistency with item fourteen as grounds, and said nothing else. Not where she'd stood. Not whose land it was. Not what it had cost her mother to be given the reason instead of the rule.

It was not the whole truth. It was, she told herself, checking the sentence the way she checked every sentence before she filed it, not a lie either. A Line Captain reviewing a fleet-wide bulletin had flagged a coordinate on the strength of a documented marker. That this Line Captain also happened, privately, to know exactly what stood on that coordinate was not a fact the flag required her to disclose.

She was aware this was the second time in ten days she had drawn that particular line. She was aware, too, that a line drawn twice in the same place was starting to look less like an exception and more like a policy.

She filed it anyway. That was, increasingly, simply what the job looked like from here.

:::pov wender

Wender had learned to read Shepherd's silences somewhere around the woman's third year as a Senior, back when "Senior" had still meant Wender only saw her twice a season instead of across a shared duty roster. She had not expected, at the cadet stage, to end up reading anyone that carefully. Command had taught her otherwise, the way command taught most useful things: by making the alternative more expensive.

She watched Shepherd go through item sixteen twice.

Most Line Captains under fleet-inspection review skimmed a forty-page bulletin the way it was meant to be skimmed — for anything actionable, nothing more. Shepherd read item sixteen the way she read anything she intended to stand behind later: slowly, once for the content and once for what the content implied, and then she flagged it, cited her grounds in four clean lines, and moved to item seventeen without a visible change in pace.

The pace was the tell. Wender had commanded long enough to know that the absence of a reaction, held that precisely, was itself a reaction.

She did not ask what Shepherd knew about item sixteen that the bulletin didn't say. She had considered asking, briefly, in the particular way a High Captain considers most things she decides against: not out of incuriosity, but because she had long ago worked out which questions improved an operation and which ones only satisfied the asker. *What aren't you telling me* rarely did the former. It was, more often than she liked to admit about her own service, simply a superior officer spending a subordinate's trust to relieve her own uncertainty.

The flag was sound. The grounds were documented. The survey request would go out fleet-priority regardless of whatever Shepherd was carrying that she hadn't put in writing, and Wender had built a command philosophy, across four seasons of watching a former cadet outpace her own cohort by a rank and a half, on exactly that distinction: an order was correct if it was correct given what was currently known, not if the officer giving it had disclosed everything she privately knew. Demanding the second as a condition of trusting the first was, in Wender's experience, how you taught good officers to stop bringing you the truths that mattered and start bringing you only the ones that were easy to explain.

"Fleet priority on sixteen," she said instead, initialing the flag. "Good catch."

"Thank you, ma'am."

Shepherd's voice gave away exactly nothing further, which told Wender, more than any confession could have, precisely how much there was underneath it.

She let it go. For now. A High Captain who let a good officer keep her reasons, Wender had found, generally got the officer's judgment intact when it finally mattered — and something in the set of Shepherd's shoulders over item sixteen suggested that whatever this was, it was not going to stay quiet forever.
