:::pov tissadelle

The morning came up over the Boirinn Uplands the way it had come up every morning of her childhood: grey first, then the particular gold that only the Union's home star threw, then the smell of the flock somewhere below the house. She stood at the low wall her great-grandmother had built and let the light find her, and it was so exactly right that she noticed, distantly, that she had not decided to come here.

That was the first thing. She filed it the way she filed everything — without alarm, because alarm was a response you gave before you understood the problem, and she had never seen the use of it. She simply noted: *I do not remember arriving.* And then, because the note asked for it: *I do not remember leaving Threshold.*

The wall was cold under her hand. She could feel the grain of the stone, the lichen, the give where the third course had shifted a century before anyone thought to reset it. All of it was there. None of it was thin. If this was a memory she was walking through, it was a memory with more in it than she had ever put into one.

She looked down at the hill and could not, for a moment, find the coordinate string. Item sixteen. The unfiled Hollow, the phase-lag in the leaves, the thing she had chosen not to write down. It should have been there — the hill was here, entire, and the Hollow was part of the hill. But when she reached for the exact place her mother had walked her to, the place with the rule and no reason, the hillside offered her the place readily, too readily, and offered it twice, at two different distances from the house, and she stood holding both and understood that a hillside is not supposed to be able to do that.

*A real hill,* she thought, in the flat interior voice she used for reports she intended to stand behind, *has one of everything.*

She did not panic. She was aware that this was itself worth noting — that a person standing on a hill that had just produced two of the same clearing might reasonably be expected to feel something sharper than professional interest. She felt professional interest. She wondered, with the part of her that never fully turned off, whether that was because she was steady, or because whatever she was standing in did not yet contain enough of her to be afraid with.

Below her, the door of the house opened, and her mother came out to take in the morning, exactly as her mother had, and Tissadelle went down the hill to meet her, because that was what you did, and because she wanted to very badly, and because she had not yet worked out that wanting something this much was, here, a kind of evidence.

:::pov orla

Órla Shepherd had buried three generations from that doorstep and expected, in the ordinary way of things, to be buried from it herself. She was not a woman given to visions. The land did not talk to her; it only kept, the way land keeps, and the women of the holding had always known the difference between a place that held something and a place that spoke, and had never once confused the two.

So when her daughter came down the hill to her that morning, Órla knew two things at the same time and did not let either one show, because showing them would have been a way of deciding which was true before she had the right to.

The first: this was her daughter. The set of her, the way she came down a slope she had come down ten thousand times, favouring the same ankle she'd turned at nine. No one else moved like that. No copy would think to.

The second: her daughter was not here. Tiss was off with the Rangers, had been for years, would send word before she came, always did, had never once in her life appeared on the hill unannounced at first light with the look of someone who had walked a long way in the dark and come out somewhere she recognised.

Órla had spent a lifetime declining to smooth over the things that did not fit. It was, though she would never have said it in these words, the family's oldest work — older than the flock, older than the name. You kept the rule about the Hollow and you did not invent a reason for it, because the reason you invented would be a lie, and a lie kept on the land was worse than a silence kept on it. So she looked at the girl who was and was not her daughter, and she did the hardest thing the holding had ever taught anyone to do.

She let both be true. She put the kettle on. And she said, in the voice she'd have used any ordinary morning, "You're up early," and watched to see whether the answer came back the way an answer comes from a person, or the way it comes from a room that has been waiting to be asked.

It came back like her daughter. That was the part that frightened her, later, when she was able to be frightened. Not that the thing on her step was a stranger.

That it wasn't.
