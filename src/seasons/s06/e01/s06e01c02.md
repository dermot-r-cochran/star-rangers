:::pov wender

The citation was good work. That was the problem with it.

Wender had read enough of them to know the difference between a document written to record what happened and one written to make what happened easy to stop thinking about, and the draft on her desk was a masterpiece of the second kind. *Line Captain T. Shepherd held the boundary at Dock Seven through the completion of evacuation, at the cost of her own recovery. All personnel present survived. The Oversight Council recognises —* and so on, down to the part where recognising her became a way of filing her, and filing her became a way of never again asking why a forty-second chronometer discrepancy had been allowed to sit on this station's books for eleven years while whatever it actually was got quietly larger.

Everything in it was true. She checked, the way Shepherd would have checked, once for content and once for what the content was for. Every sentence was true and the sum of the sentences was a lie, and the lie was: *this is finished.*

She did not sign it. She was aware that not signing it cost her something she could not yet name and would be billed for later — the Council did not love a High Captain who left its files open, and it had ways of remembering. She weighed that honestly, because pretending a cost was zero was how you ended up paying it by surprise. Then she set the stylus down.

An order was sound if it was correct given what was currently known. She had built a career on that sentence, and Shepherd had built one alongside her out of the same material. What was currently known was that a woman had walked into a boundary rupture and the station had recovered no body, and that "no body" and "confirmed loss" were being asked to mean the same thing by people who very much needed a Tuesday where this was over.

*No body* was not, she happened to know from a career spent at the edges where instruments failed, a synonym for *gone.* It was a synonym for *we did not get everything back,* which was a different and much larger sentence, and she was not going to let the Council collapse the two just because the smaller one closed faster.

She keyed the internal line to the one member of her survey team who had never in its operational life mistaken the absence of a reading for the absence of a thing.

"Rook. Dock Seven. What's still there."

:::pov rook

**Field Analyst Rook-7. Log open. Query received from High Captain Wender, 0416 station: *what is still there.* Preliminary note appended to own follow-up queue: the High Captain asked what is still there, not whether anything is. The wording is doing work. Logging the wording.**

Dock Seven read, to the station's certified instruments, as a boundary failure decaying on schedule. Rook-7 did not dispute the certified instruments. It simply carried others, of an origin it had flagged twice in its own records and been asked about zero times, and those others had been declining to agree with the certified ones for nineteen days.

A total loss at a rupture point produced a field like a struck bell going quiet: energetic, then less so, then a smooth nothing, monotone all the way down. Rook-7 had logged eleven of those in its service. It knew the shape the way it knew its own chassis weight.

Dock Seven was not going quiet. Dock Seven was going *warm* — not in temperature, though the maintenance crews had noticed the temperature too and filed it under a fault in the heat exchange and been, Rook-7 assessed, entirely wrong. Warm in the sense that a structured, low-amplitude signal was persisting at the failure point where a struck bell should have long since stopped ringing, and the structure was not decaying. It was *iterating.* Something at Dock Seven was repeating itself with small variations, the way a system repeats itself when it is trying to hold a pattern it cannot quite complete and will not let drop.

**Assessment, flagged for the High Captain and no one above her: this signature is inconsistent with total loss. It is consistent, at low confidence, with a stalled process that is still running.**

Rook-7 logged a second observation and did not send it, because it had not yet decided whether the observation was analysis or the other thing its logs sometimes did that it had never resolved the status of. The observation was this: the iteration at Dock Seven had a rhythm, and the rhythm was not mechanical. It phrased. It repeated and varied and returned, the way a thing repeats and varies and returns when it is not a machine cycling but a mind going over and over the same ground looking for the way out.

It had heard that once before, in the archived Sentinel telemetry a Principal named Shepherd had refused to file as noise.

**Note. The Line Captain taught this station to stop reading a phrasing pattern as static. It would be consistent — and Rook-7 logs that consistency is not proof — for the station to now be receiving one from her.**

Rook-7 forwarded the field data to the Survey Archive, addressed to the desk that had spent eleven years reopening files the rest of the station had agreed to close. If anyone would put the warm signal next to the right other thing and let the adjacency argue, it was Sen.

**Log held open. The bell is not going quiet. Recommend the High Captain be told, in these words: it is still ringing, and it is ringing on purpose.**

:::
