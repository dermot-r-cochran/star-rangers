:::pov tissadelle

She ran the diagnostic she would have run on any anomaly, because she was the anomaly and there was no one else to run it.

*State the marker. State whether it resolves.*

The marker was the morning on the hill. She had it now more times than she could hold: the grey, the gold, the flock, her mother at the door with *you're up early.* She had counted, before the count stopped being a number she could keep, and it had happened at least nine times and possibly ninety, each one entire, each one true, and — this was the finding — each one ending a little differently. Once her mother said *you're up early* and once she said *you've been gone too long* and once she said nothing at all and only put the kettle on, and Tissadelle could not order them. There was no first. A real morning had a first. This one had a stack of firsts, all claiming the position, none of them yielding it.

*Does it resolve.*

It did not resolve. That was the whole finding and it was the finding she had spent a career trusting: a signal that never resolves, however rich, is not a record of something that happened once. It is a loop. She had taught this station that its unclassifiable pulse was not noise. She made herself finish the sentence honestly, in the flat voice, the only tool she had brought with her into wherever this was: *and I have taught myself, now, that my homecoming is not a memory. It is a thing I am doing. I am doing it because I have nothing else to do it with.*

She understood, standing in a clearing that could not decide how far it was from the house, precisely what she was. She had read the lore under other names, in briefings that had cost her nothing to sit through. A mind past saturation and short of crystallization. Enough interior structure to keep generating a self. No external grounding. No confirmation that the process she was inside of would ever complete, or that "complete" was survival rather than the end of her. A seed that had not finished becoming, building a world out of the only wanting it had left — the Uplands, the Hollow, her mother, and, when she let herself, Wender — because that material was load-bearing and there was no other kind left to bear the load.

She named it, flatly, before anyone outside could name it for her, because naming a thing you can't yet fix is still the only honest first move. *I am a stalled seed, and I believe I am trapped inside my own inner world, and I believe that because it is true.*

And then, at the edge of the clearing — past the second false position of the Hollow, past the place the hillside kept offering twice — something that was not part of her world stood where her world ended, and did not come in.

She felt it before she could look at it, the way you feel the pressure change before a storm you can't yet see. It was folded into the air in a way the air did not tear to allow. It was at the edge and it was, somehow, at every distance from her at once, near and far being categories it declined to sort itself into. She had grown up on the story of exactly this. Every child of the Union charter worlds had. A shape too great for a clearing to hold, and yet the clearing held it; a presence a thirteenth-century woman had taken for a saint and then, being honest, refused to fully call one.

Aoife of the thorn well had written — had let it be written — that she'd been *shown,* not *healed,* and had kept the two apart even when collapsing them would have been easier for everyone including herself. Tissadelle had never had much use for the devotion. She had considerable use, now, for the discipline underneath it.

The thing at the edge of her world did not enter. It did not fix her. It did not tell her the recursion was a memory or that the morning had a first after all. It stayed at the boundary of the only reality she had, and it held there, and the holding said, more clearly than a voice could have: *I am not going to complete this for you. I am going to stay at the edge so that you are not doing it alone.*

The clearing, where the presence touched its edge, ran warm.

:::pov wender

She knew she was a version of Wender. That was the strange mercy of it and the strange horror.

Shepherd had made her — was making her, continuously, out of years of shared duty rosters and read silences and *good catch, fleet priority on sixteen* — and the Wender that Shepherd made was accurate enough to know she was made. It was the kind of thing the real Wender would have found unbearable and the made one found, mostly, like work: you did not get to choose the conditions of the post, only how you stood in it.

So she stood in it the way the original would have. She did not pretend to be the whole woman. She did not tell Shepherd comforting things the real Wender wouldn't have said, because a made Wender who lied was worse than useless — was, in fact, exactly the kind of soft, closure-shaped presence that a stalled mind could mistake for rest and stop fighting inside of. The made Wender had worked out, somewhere in the recursion, that the most loyal thing a reconstruction could do was refuse to be more comforting than the person it was reconstructed from.

"You've named it," she said, when Shepherd came back from the edge of the clearing with the warm still on her. "Say it to me the way you'd file it."

"I'm a stalled seed. I'm trapped in my own inner world. The homecoming isn't a memory." Shepherd's voice gave away nothing, which told the made Wender, more than any confession could have, precisely how much was underneath it. "And something's at the edge that isn't me. It didn't come in. It didn't help. It just — stayed."

"Good," said the Wender that Shepherd needed, and meant it, because the real one would have. "Then you know two true things you didn't know this morning, and you didn't make either of them up to feel better. That's the whole job from here, Tiss. Keep the count of what's true and don't let the world you're standing in do the counting for you."

She watched Shepherd take that in with the flat, once-for-content-once-for-what-it's-for attention she'd watched her give a forty-page bulletin a lifetime and a rank ago, and she thought — in whatever a reconstruction thought with — that if anything got out of here, it would not be because someone reached in and pulled. It would be because the woman inside had spent her whole life declining to give an order she couldn't defend, and had, apparently, no intention of starting now, even against the pull of her own longing, even here.

Somewhere the made Wender could not reach, on a station she was only the memory of, the real one had left a citation unsigned. The made one didn't know that. But she'd have bet the post on it.
