:::pov syra

Syra did not experience deep fear, which made her, on a station full of people trying not to hope, the correct person to hold the boundary data. Hope distorted a signal the way fear did — by deciding in advance what it wanted the signal to be. She wanted nothing from Dock Seven except its actual value, and so she was the one who could read the warm signature and say the two things about it that everyone else kept collapsing into one.

"It is structured," she told the room, which was Wender and the desk that Sen kept. "And it is being sustained. Those are separate findings. A thing can be structured because it is alive, and it can be sustained because something is feeding it from outside, and those are not the same claim, and I will not let you treat the first as proof of the second or the second as proof of the first."

This was the discipline, and it was Ranger doctrine before it was Syra's temperament, though her temperament made it cheaper for her than for most: *name what you know.* Not what you fear. Not what would close the file. What you can actually support.

What she could support: the signal at the failure point had the profile of a stalled process, past saturation, iterating without resolving — consistent with a mind that had not finished becoming something the setting had a name and a governance structure for. What she could support: the warm anomaly matched nothing in the boundary-decay archive, and matched, when Sen laid the two records adjacent, an instrument note eight centuries old — a well in Ireland that ran warm for three days after a woman met something at it that the Union's own scholars now read as a Levril, working exactly inside the limits its kind worked inside, folding into a place without tearing it.

What she could *not* yet support, and refused to pretend she could: whether the reason the process had not resolved was the ordinary physics of an incomplete transference, or a [Gilded Saint](/star-rangers/glossary/gilded-saints/) holding the closure open because a stalled, sanctified, never-quite-finished sacrifice was precisely what such a thing fed on — or the Council's citation doing the entity's work for free, keeping the file shut and the story beautiful and the question closed, with no entity present at all.

"All three are real hazards," she said. "The doctrine for the last two is the same instinct. Precision. But they are not interchangeable, and if we name a Saint that is not there we will have done, ourselves, the exact imprecise thing the doctrine exists to prevent. So we rule out before we name. In order. Starting with the one that costs nothing to check and would embarrass us most to have missed: our own paperwork."

:::pov wender

Wender had read the doctrine on Court-Fae and Gilded Saints the way she read everything, once for content and once for what it was for, and the thing it was for had always struck her as almost too simple to be a weapon: *name what you know, discharge the debt, recognise the closure-prevention pattern, accept that enough is enough.* You did not fight the thing that fed on an unfinished story. You finished the story accurately, and it starved.

She had not expected, when she declined to sign the citation, to be practising counter-doctrine against a threat that might turn out to be her own institution. But Syra had put it plainly, and Syra did not flinch things into being softer than they were, and the plain version was this: the Oversight Council's hero's citation and a Gilded Saint's feeding did the same work. Both kept the file shut. Both turned Shepherd from a woman into a meaning. Both made it impossible to ask, ever again, what had actually happened at Dock Seven, because asking would insult the sacrifice — and *you must not reopen the sacrifice* was the exact sentence a closure-prevention entity most wanted the room to believe, whether or not any entity was in the room to want it.

"So we assume nothing sacred," Wender said. "We assume an open incident with a survivor we haven't recovered, and we write the accurate account, and we find out which of three things has been keeping it open by the simple method of doing the one thing every version of the closure would prefer we didn't."

Sen's desk produced, without editorial, the eight-hundred-year-old note and the nineteen-day-old field data, laid adjacent, and let the adjacency argue the way Sen always did. Wender looked at a well running warm across eight centuries and a boundary running warm across nineteen days and understood that whatever was at the edge of wherever Shepherd had gone was not a metaphor and not a mercy and not a monster. It was a Cascade entity that had done this before, at a thorn well, for a woman who'd had the honesty to say she couldn't fully name it — and it was, if the archive meant anything, still doing the only thing its kind was permitted to do: standing at the edge, holding, refusing to leave, unable to reach in and finish it for her.

"It can't pull her out," Wender said slowly, working it the way Shepherd would have. "Any more than it pulled Aoife anywhere. It can only keep the channel from closing. Which means whatever gets her out — if anything does — has to come from her, or from us, or from both. Not from the thing holding the door."

"Correct," said Syra, who found this neither hopeful nor hopeless, only true. "Which is why the account matters. It is not a eulogy. It is the rope. If we name what happened precisely enough, we starve whatever is feeding on it staying vague — and we give the woman on the other side of that warm edge one true thing to hold that she did not have to build herself out of her own memory. She has been generating every certainty she has. We can send her one she doesn't have to make up."

Wender picked up the stylus she had set down nineteen days ago, and for the first time since Dock Seven, wrote a sentence about Tissadelle Shepherd that was neither a citation nor a loss. She wrote what was known. It was a short list. She intended to make it longer, and true, and to keep it open exactly as long as the truth required and not one Tuesday less.
