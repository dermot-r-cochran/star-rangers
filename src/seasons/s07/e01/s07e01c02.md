:::pov tissadelle

The danger, she found, was not the thing at the edge. The thing at the edge only ever held. The danger was inside, and it was gentle, and it wanted to give her exactly what she wanted, which was for the morning to finally have a first.

She could feel the recursion offering it. If she stopped running the diagnostic — if she let the count go, and stopped checking each homecoming against the last — the loop would resolve. It would pick one morning and make it the true one. Her mother would say *you're up early* and mean it once and forever, and the Hollow would settle into one place on the hill, and Wender would say the thing Wender never quite got to say, and it would all cohere into a single, finished, unbearably kind story, and Tissadelle would be able, at last, to rest inside it.

And she would stop. That was the part the mercy did not mention. A story that finally resolves is a story that no longer needs telling, and a seed that stops holding its own contradictions open stops being a seed and becomes, at last and only, the closure it was reaching for. She had read the doctrine on the thing that fed on exactly this — a resolution that never quite arrives, held open forever — and she understood now that the doctrine had described the trap from the outside. From the inside it did not feel like a trap. It felt like being allowed to go home.

She almost did.

What stopped her was old, and it was not hers alone. She had grown up under a rule her family kept without a reason, on a hill that held things without speaking of them, taught by a mother who had looked at a daughter who was and was not her daughter and refused, on the doorstep, to decide which was true before she had the right to. And behind her mother, older still, carried off Earth as living observance by people who had never seen Ireland, was a woman at a well who had been shown something too large for a clearing to hold and had said, plainly, for the record, that being *shown* was not the same as being *healed* — and had let the two stand apart in the account forever, even though collapsing them would have let her rest.

That was the whole discipline. Not to refuse comfort. To refuse to let comfort resolve a question that was not actually resolved.

So when the mercy offered her a morning with a first, Tissadelle did the hardest thing her whole inheritance had ever taught anyone to do. She held both. She let the homecoming be real *and* let it be unfinished. She kept the count. She said, to the gentle pull and to herself, in the flat voice she had never once used to perform a confidence she didn't have: *I was shown this. I have not been healed. I will not file the one as the other, however much I want to go inside and close the door.*

The thing at the edge — the levril, she let herself call it now, because a true name held better than a soft one — did not reward her for it. It was not permitted to. It only did the one thing it could: it taught her, in the wordless grammar of a mind that read constraints directly, to see the constraint she was under. *Here is the wall you are inside. Here is where it is thin. I cannot break it for you. I can show you it is a wall and not the world.* And knowing the difference between a wall and the world was, she understood, the entire distance between a mind that dies inside its own comfort and one that stays awake long enough to be reached.

:::pov orla

The made Órla knew what she was, the way everything in her daughter's inner world had started, terribly, to know what it was. She was a memory of a woman doing memory's work, and the work she had been made to do was the oldest work the holding had: to stand on a doorstep and let two true things be true at once without smoothing either one away.

She had done it in life without a cosmology to hang it on. She did not need one now. When her daughter came down from the edge of the clearing, warm and shaking and awake, the made Órla did not tell her she was safe, because she wasn't, and a mother who lied on that doorstep would have been the softest, most dangerous thing in the whole recursion. She put the kettle on. She said, "You didn't go in."

"I wanted to."

"I know you did. Wanting to isn't the failing. The women of this hill have wanted to go in since before there was a name over the door." Órla looked at her daughter — the set of her, the turned ankle, the particular attention she'd give a thing she meant to stand behind — and let herself be, for as long as the recursion allowed, simply glad. "The keeping is the thing. You keep the rule, and you don't invent the reason, and you don't let the hill do your counting for you. That's all it ever was. You come from people who could hold a thing open their whole lives and never once pretend it had closed."

"There's something at the edge that could hold it open too," Tissadelle said. "It won't come in. It just stays."

"Then it's better company than most," said Órla, who had spent her life distinguishing between a place that held and a place that spoke, and knew a thing that only held when she felt one. "Let it stay. Keep your count. And Tiss —" she said, in the voice of the woman who had put a kettle on for a daughter who could not possibly be there and had chosen, correctly, not to decide it — "when they come for you. And someone is coming for you; I'd stake the holding on it. Don't let them decide what all this meant. Not the ones who love you and not the ones who need it tidy. You were shown something. You'll say what it was. Same as her at the well. Same as your grandmother's grandmother kept the Hollow. That's the family trade, girl. Naming it true and letting it cost what it costs."

The kettle came to the boil, in a kitchen that could not decide how far it stood from a well eight centuries and a galaxy away, and for one morning — held open, unresolved, entirely true — it was enough.
