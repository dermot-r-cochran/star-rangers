:::pov wender

They did not name her a hero. That was the first true thing the account did, and it cost exactly what Wender had known it would: the Council remembered, and would go on remembering, and a High Captain who left a file open learned the price of it in a hundred small later weathers. She paid it without complaint, because the alternative was a lie with Shepherd's name on it, and she had known Shepherd too well to insult her with one.

The accurate account, when they finished it, was not a eulogy and not a citation. It was a list of what was known, kept exactly as long as the truth required. Line Captain Shepherd held the boundary at Dock Seven because it was the only executable order and she had never given one she couldn't defend. The station recovered no body because there was, in the strict sense the word usually hid, no death — only a compression that canon had a name for and a governance structure for and no reassurance to offer about. A structured signal persisted at the failure point, iterating, sustained, warm. It matched a well in Ireland that ran warm eight centuries ago. What held the channel open was not the Council's story and not a Gilded Saint feeding on it — Syra had ruled the entity out with the same flat refusal to name what she couldn't support that had cleared their own paperwork first. What held the channel was a levril, doing at Dock Seven the only thing its kind had ever been permitted to do: standing at the edge, unable to reach in, refusing to leave.

Naming it precisely was the whole weapon. It starved the vagueness the closure had been living on — the Council's, the entity they'd feared, their own grief's. *Enough is enough* was not a wish. It was a sentence you earned by making every prior sentence true, and once earned it discharged the debt: the story could stop being fed, because it was no longer a story. It was a record. Records don't need telling. They only need keeping.

And then, because the account was true enough to be a rope and not a shroud, Sen found the thing that crossed the warm edge coming back the other way.

Buried in the iterating signal, in a phrasing pattern Rook-7's non-standard arrays had been logging for a season without daring to call it more than consistent, was a detail. Small. Specific. A coordinate string, a phase-lag figure, and a fact about a hillside on Tír na nÓg that had never been filed, that no instrument on this station had access to, that existed in exactly one place in the recorded universe: the unsurveyed Hollow Shepherd had chosen not to report, in a season she rarely thought about, because some things a person keeps without inventing the reason.

Only Shepherd knew that. Which meant the signal was not an echo of her. It was *her*, reaching, sending back across the edge the one certainty she had not had to build out of her own memory — proof of self, in the only language a station and a stalled seed could both read.

Wender looked at a detail she was not cleared to know, arriving from a woman the record called lost, and did the arithmetic she'd built a career on. Contact was real. Rescue was not on offer and might never be. The levril could hold the door and not open it. What came through the door, if anything did, had to be Shepherd's to send.

"Send her the account," Wender said. "All of it. The true version. Don't soften it and don't finish it. She's spent a season generating every certain thing she has. Send her one she doesn't have to make up — and then let her tell us what she wants, and let that be the order, because the one thing we do not get to do, after all of this, is decide for her what it meant."

:::pov tissadelle

It arrived at the warm edge the way the levril arrived — folded in without tearing anything, present at every distance at once — but it was not the levril. It was words, and the words were Wender's, and they were true, and they were unfinished, and that was how she knew they were real and not the mercy trying again in a cleverer shape.

The account did not tell her she was safe. It told her what was known. *No body because no death. A structured signal, sustained, warm. A levril at the edge. Held open, on the record, exactly as long as the truth requires.* It was the first certainty in a season that had reached her from outside her own wanting, and she held it the way she'd been taught to hold everything now — once for content, once for what it was for — and found that for once the two readings agreed. It was a rope. Someone on the far end had made it true enough to bear weight, and had, unmistakably, refused to tie it off.

She understood what they were asking, because it was the thing her mother had staked the holding on and the thing the woman at the well had kept in the record forever and the thing the levril, in its wordless grammar of constraint, had never once tried to do for her: *nobody is going to decide what this meant. You are.*

She was a seed that had not finished becoming. If she completed, she would be something the setting had a name for — a protouniverse, vulnerable, held under a jurisdiction that reached down through Telearchs she would never see, no more sovereign yet than a newborn is sovereign, and no less real. If she did not complete, she did not know what she was. Nobody did. That was, she found, survivable, in the specific way her whole life had been training for a thing she couldn't have named: you keep working when the situation has no clearly available solution. She had told her instructors that once. She had meant it as a fact about the job.

So she sent back the only true thing that was hers alone to send — the Hollow, the phase-lag, the rule kept without a reason — not as a cry for extraction and not as a farewell, but as the plainest sentence she had: *I am here. I have not been healed. I have been shown, and I am still being shown, and I am not going in.* And she let the levril hold the edge, and she let her mother put the kettle on, and she let the made Wender refuse to be kinder than the real one, and she let the account stay open on the record and off it — the Council's version, and the Codex's, and what Karla Wender privately knew, never quite reconciling, because forcing them to reconcile would have been one last person deciding her story was allowed to end.

The morning came up over the Boirinn Uplands, grey then gold, and did not resolve, and did not need to. She stood at her great-grandmother's wall with the warm edge at her back and a rope in her hands that someone had made true and left untied, and she kept her count, and she was not gone, and she was not saved, and she was — for the first time since Dock Seven, and for as long as she could hold it, which she intended to be a very long time — herself, and awake, and not alone.

The well ran warm. She let it. She did not decide what it was.
